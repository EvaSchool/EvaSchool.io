Using Newton’s Laws

Tension and Newton’s Laws

     

Two masses are tied together with a string, and a horizontally applied force pulls m1 to the right. If the string exerts a tension of 20 N [left ] on m1, then it also exerts a tension of 20 N [right] on m2. 

This is a direct consequence of Newton’s third law and the fact that the string does not stretch and has negligible mass.

How can we measure tension directly?

      

  

One way is with a force sensor or a spring scale. If you tie opposite ends of a string to two separate force sensors and then pull on the string in opposite directions, you can easily see that the readings on both sensors are the same. 

Again, this is a direct consequence of Newton’s third law and our assumptions about strings.

  

  

A single force sensor or spring scale can be used to measure the tension in strings if it is tied between two strings.

(b) In this diagram, the force of gravity has not changed, and the object is at rest. This means that the FBD is the same, and we will find the same tension. This example reinforces the concept that pulleys only change the direction of force without changing the magnitude of the force.

  

In this balanced system, both objects are at rest. By drawing an FBD for either object, you will get the same result for the tension. This result is different from what most people would expect. Most people would incorrectly say the string holds up twice as much mass and should have twice the tension. 

Others mistakenly think the tension is zero since both forces of gravity pull the string at each end, and they should cancel. Neither statement is true. The second object provides the force necessary to hold up the first object. In other words, the second object is just doing the job of the wall or beam, but otherwise, the situation is unchanged.

(a) Determine the acceleration of the sleds.

(b) Calculate the magnitude of the tension in rope A.

(A) Determine the acceleration of the sleds.(B) Calculate the magnitude of the tension in the rope.

  

All three sleds will move together with the same acceleration, so we can treat them as one single object. The total mass of all three sleds is mT = 12.0 kg + 11.0 kg + 7.0 kg = 30.0 kg.

There is no need to consider the tension at this point because it is an internal force and will not contribute to the acceleration of the total mass. Choose east as positive. So west is negative.

  

The acceleration of all three sleds is 4.0 m/s2 [E].

  

We could use the FBD for either sled 2 or sled 3 to calculate the tension in rope A. We will use the FBD for sled 3. Because it is slightly simpler—it only has rope A pulling on it, whereas sled 2 has both rope A and rope B pulling on it.
