Social Norms

The word ‘norm’ refers to standards or patterns, and it can be used in a variety of contexts. 

Social scientists study behavior, and therefore when discussing norms in social science, they are referred to as social norms.

‘Social norms’ refer to expected patterns of behavior in particular situations and may vary by culture and change over time.


For instance, it would be expected that brides in Canadian culture would wear white on their wedding day. The social norm in China, however, would be for a bride to wear a red gown. 


In Canadian society today, communication norms have changed, and texting has become more socially acceptable.


If you were to communicate with someone, the social norm indicates that you would likely text before picking up the phone to make a call.

Social Roles

Roles are the proper or customary functions that a person or institution performs. 

  • Social roles can refer to our duties and responsibilities or positions we hold in society. 
    We all have many different roles in life. For instance, you are a student, but perhaps you are also an employee, a member of a religious faith, or a player on a sports team. 
  • You also have unique duties and responsibilities that you uphold for each of the roles you play.

For example, a coach is a role. Coaches set up a practice schedule for players, communicate with parents, and assist athletes in developing their skills.

 

Parenting is another role that has many responsibilities. Parents must feed and clothe their children, provide discipline, as well as provide love and guidance.

 

  • Social norms are the unwritten rules and expectations that govern the behavior of individuals within a specific society or group. These norms shape and regulate various aspects of human interaction, influencing everything from communication styles to personal space boundaries. Understanding social norms is crucial for individuals to navigate and integrate successfully into their communities. Here, we'll explore the concept of social norms in greater detail, covering their definition, functions, development, and impact on society.
  • Social norms refer to the shared expectations, beliefs, and behaviors that are considered appropriate and acceptable within a particular cultural or social context. These norms help maintain order and cohesion within a society by providing a framework for social interactions and relationships.

Functions of Social Norms:

Open All Panels

Social Order

  1. Norms contribute to the establishment of social order by providing a set of guidelines that govern behavior. This helps prevent chaos and ensures that individuals can predict and understand the actions of others.

Identity Formation

  1. Social norms play a significant role in shaping individual and group identities. Adherence to certain norms helps people define themselves within their social context and reinforces a sense of belonging.

Socialization

  1. Norms are transmitted through the process of socialization, wherein individuals learn the values, customs, and behaviors deemed appropriate in their society. Family, education, and media all contribute to this process.

Conflict Resolution

  1. Norms provide a basis for resolving conflicts and disagreements. When individuals share common expectations, they can use these as a reference point for resolving disputes and maintaining social harmony.

Development of Social Norms:

Open All Panels

Cultural Transmission

  1. Social norms are often passed down from generation to generation through cultural traditions, rituals, and shared experiences. This transmission ensures continuity in social expectations.

Institutional Influence

  1. Institutions such as family, education, religion, and government play a crucial role in shaping and reinforcing social norms. These institutions provide a structured environment for individuals to learn and internalize societal expectations.

Social Influence

  1. Peer pressure and social influence contribute to the formation and reinforcement of social norms. Individuals may conform to certain behaviors to fit in with their peers or avoid social rejection.

Impact on Society:

Open All Panels

Conformity and Deviance

  1. Social norms create a standard against which individuals measure their behavior. Conformity to these norms is rewarded, while deviation may lead to social sanctions. This dynamic helps maintain order and cohesion in society.

Cultural Diversity

  1. Different societies have distinct sets of social norms, contributing to cultural diversity. Norms reflect the values and priorities of a particular community, shaping its unique identity.

Social Change

  1. Over time, social norms can evolve and change in response to shifts in societal values, technological advancements, or cultural movements. Activism and advocacy can also challenge and reshape existing norms.


In conclusion, social norms are a fundamental aspect of human society, providing a framework for social order, identity formation, and cultural transmission. Understanding and navigating these norms are essential for individuals to participate effectively in their communities and contribute to the ongoing evolution of societal expectations.

Social Institutions

  • Social institutions are used to describe groups of people who are banded together for a common goal or purpose. That purpose generally involves giving members rights and privileges. 
  • In society, many different social institutions help ensure that things run smoothly.

To make it a bit more simple, think of society as a car. The social institutions are all the parts that make up the car and make sure that it functions properly. 

 

Without wheels, a car may still run, but you won’t get very far! As with the car, all parts work in conjunction with each other to make society function more smoothly.

Social Institutions

  • There are many social institutions necessary to make society function and run well. 
  • The political system, justice system, educational system, and family, economy, and religion are all examples of social institutions. They each have a unique function.
  • For instance, the justice system functions to uphold laws and order in society. It is composed of courts, judges, and police.

Social institutions are complex, organized systems within society that fulfill essential functions and contribute to the structure and stability of a community. These institutions are pervasive and influence various aspects of individuals' lives, shaping behavior, norms, and interactions. Understanding social institutions is crucial for comprehending the dynamics and functioning of societies. Let's delve deeper into the concept of social institutions, exploring their definition, types, functions, and significance.

Types of Social Institutions:

Open All Panels

Family Institution

The family is a fundamental social institution that involves a group of individuals connected by blood, marriage, or adoption.

  • Functions: The family provides emotional support, socialization, and the necessities for its members. It plays a crucial role in transmitting cultural values and norms.

Educational Institution

 Education institutions are structures designed to impart knowledge, skills, and cultural values to individuals.

  • Functions: Education prepares individuals for societal roles, fosters critical thinking, and contributes to social mobility by providing opportunities for personal and professional development.

Religious Institution

Religious institutions involve organized systems of beliefs, practices, and rituals centered around a higher power or spiritual principles.

  • Functions: Religion often provides a moral framework, ethical guidelines, and a sense of purpose. It can also contribute to social cohesion and community bonding.

Governmental Institution

Governmental institutions encompass the structures and systems that manage and regulate societal affairs.

  • Functions: Governments provide law and order, protect citizens' rights, and create policies for the collective welfare. They play a vital role in maintaining social order and justice.

Economic Institution

Economic institutions involve systems of production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services within a society.

  • Functions: Economic institutions contribute to the allocation of resources, job creation, and the overall prosperity of a community. They also influence social stratification and mobility.

Healthcare Institution

Healthcare institutions include organized systems that provide medical care and services to individuals.

  • Functions: Healthcare institutions contribute to the well-being of society by promoting and maintaining the health of individuals. They play a critical role in disease prevention, treatment, and public health.
