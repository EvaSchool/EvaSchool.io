From Teen to Independent Adult

Leaving Home

During the transition to adulthood there are many changes that take place. Leaving home for the first time is often a turning point or rite of passage for many young adults in Canada to signify that their role from dependent child to independent adult has occurred. However, the process of leaving home and the reasons for leaving are different for many individuals.

Costs and Benefits of Leaving Home

  • In Canada, at 16 years of age an individual can legally leave home without the permission of a parent or guardian. As you have discussed, individuals have different reasons for leaving home. 
  • When someone leaves their family home for the first time, there are many considerations that must be made. Individuals must be prepared to face a number of adjustments when leaving home.

The Transition to Adulthood: New Challenges

Factors influencing the decision to leave home have changed over time. Some social scientists argue that Canadian adolescents are facing more challenges today, as there are many issues that did not exist in the past.

The following video clip will explain how four revolutions (technological, sexual, women’s movement and youth movement) have impacted the transition to adulthood today. Watch the following clip. Consider the title: Why does it take longer to grow up today?

According to the video, four revolutions in society impacted the transition to adulthood: 

  • the Technology Revolution
  • the Sexual Revolution
  • the Women’s Movement
  • the Youth Movement

Adolescents are not only impacted by events in society, but they are also impacted by various social institutions, such as family, media, education, religion, economy and political systems. 

Each of these social institutions will influence an individual’s thoughts and behaviors. They shape the values one has and the roles one plays in society.

  • For example, an individual might have a family that values education and therefore he/she spends all day in school and several hours after school studying.

This leaves no room for a part-time job or social activities with friends. This behavior will shape the person’s transition to adulthood. This person might spend longer in school in the role of a student, delaying potential marriage and parenting roles. The person could also stay at home longer, thus prolonging their transition to the roles associated with independent living.

  • Primary Socialization: Families are the primary socialization agents, influencing the values, norms, and behaviors that adolescents adopt. The family structure, dynamics, and parenting styles can significantly impact an adolescent's sense of identity and self-esteem.
  • Cultural Transmission: Families transmit cultural values and traditions to adolescents, contributing to the continuity of societal norms and practices.
  • Socialization and Education: Schools are key institutions for socializing adolescents. Beyond academic knowledge, they teach social skills, cultural values, and societal norms. Adolescents interact with peers and teachers, fostering social development.
  • Role in Social Mobility: Education can be a pathway for social mobility, providing adolescents with opportunities to acquire skills and knowledge that may influence their future socioeconomic status.
  • Moral and Ethical Development: Religious institutions often contribute to the moral and ethical development of adolescents. They provide a framework for understanding the world, moral guidance, and a sense of purpose.
  • Community and Identity: Adolescents may find a sense of community and identity through religious institutions, shaping their worldview and influencing their social connections.
  • Influence on Perception: Media, including social media, plays a significant role in shaping adolescents' perceptions of self and others. It can contribute to the formation of body image, self-esteem, and social expectations.
  • Cultural Representation: Media influences cultural norms and values, impacting adolescents' understanding of diversity, gender roles, and societal expectations.
  • Peer Influence: Adolescents often form close ties with peer groups, which become influential social institutions. Peer interactions can impact behavior, attitudes, and decision-making.
  • Identity Formation: Peer groups contribute to the formation of individual and group identities, helping adolescents explore their values, interests, and personal boundaries.

Understanding the intricate interplay between adolescents and social institutions is crucial for recognizing the factors that shape their development. Additionally, it highlights the reciprocal nature of influence, as adolescents, in turn, contribute to and reshape these institutions as they navigate through this critical stage of life.
