Identifying and Interpreting Data

As you have learned, social scientists attempt to examine patterns and trends in human behavior and society. To do so, they need to gather and interpret data.

Data is defined as “individual facts, statistics, or items of information.” Two main types of data can be collected and displayed to demonstrate a pattern or trend: qualitative or quantitative.

How would you describe this picture? 

Social scientists would describe the golf balls in two different ways, depending on the type of data they were gathering.

  • Qualitative data is information that describes something, or in essence, a quality of something. For example, the golf ball is white and round.
  • Quantitative data is information that defines something with a numerical value or number, or in essence a quantity of something. For example, there are 5 golf balls, and each is 4 cm in diameter.

However, social scientists do not examine things; they examine people, events, and situations. 

Therefore, qualitative data might be used if a researcher wanted to report on a person’s feelings or reactions to a situation using words.

Quantitative data would be used if they wanted to report how many people experienced something, or how trends such as birth rates have changed over time. 

For instance, if researchers had children watch an hour of television, qualitative data might be gathered on how children reacted to witnessing violence.

The quantitative data researchers may collect might be how many minutes of violent actions were viewed by the children in total.

In future lessons, you will be examining how to collect and perform research in social science. In this lesson, you are going to examine how social scientists display and interpret quantitative data to reveal patterns and trends in society.

Using Tables and Graphs

  • Basic numbers or findings can often be displayed in a chart or a table. A chart or table is made up of columns and rows and is a quick way to present relatively large quantities of data in an orderly fashion.
  • Social scientists can also display patterns and trends using graphical representations.
  • This presents the information to the readers clearly and concisely and is a bit more attention-grabbing and visually appealing than a simple table or chart.

Identifying and Interpreting Data

  • The data in the table and graph that you analysed by watching the videos presented information that should have helped you to see some patterns and trends related to the topic. 
  • As social scientists, the next step is to ask questions. Why is the data significant or important? Why might this have happened? Was something going on in society that would explain or relate to this topic?

Identifying and interpreting data represents a critical juncture in the realm of social science research, wherein the careful analysis of tables and graphs serves as the foundation for extracting meaningful insights. The visual representation of information through these mediums can illuminate patterns and trends, but the true value lies in the subsequent interrogation of the data through thoughtful and probing questions.

  •  The first layer of inquiry for social scientists involves understanding the significance or importance of the observed data. This goes beyond a mere acknowledgment of patterns; it requires a deeper exploration of why these patterns matter in the broader context of the research topic.

For example, if the data indicates a sudden surge in environmental pollution, social scientists might ask themselves why this spike is noteworthy and what implications it holds for the community, ecosystem, or public health. Unraveling the significance of the data sets the stage for a more informed and purposeful analysis.

  • Moving beyond the 'what' to the 'why' is the crux of the analytical process. Researchers must delve into the underlying causes and factors that might have contributed to the observed data patterns.
  • This involves a nuanced examination of variables, relationships, and contextual elements that might be influencing the outcomes. If the data suggests a decline in a particular economic indicator, social scientists would explore potential economic, political, or technological shifts that could explain this phenomenon. By identifying causative factors, researchers can construct a more comprehensive understanding of the dynamics at play. 
  • Moreover, the temporal and societal context is paramount in interpreting data. Social scientists must consider whether there were specific events or trends in society that coincide with the observed data patterns. This temporal analysis helps to contextualize the findings, offering insights into whether external influences or societal changes might be driving the observed trends. For instance, if the data reflects a sudden increase in public protests, researchers might investigate whether this aligns with political unrest or societal discontent.
  • The dynamic nature of society necessitates an exploration of the multifaceted dimensions surrounding the data. The next step in the analytical journey is to question whether something was happening in society that could explain or relate to the identified patterns. This broadens the scope of inquiry, prompting researchers to consider social, cultural, economic, and political factors that may be interconnected with the data trends.
  • In essence, the process of identifying and interpreting data is an intellectual expedition, requiring social scientists to move beyond the surface-level observations. By posing questions that unravel the significance, causation, and societal context, researchers can unlock the deeper meanings embedded in the data, contributing to a more nuanced and comprehensive understanding of the social phenomena under investigation.
