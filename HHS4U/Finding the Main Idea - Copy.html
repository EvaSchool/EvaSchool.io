Finding the Main Idea

Finding the Main Idea

You learned how to locate resources and determine which were suitable for inquiry from previous lessons. In this lesson, we are going to expand on that work. You will examine sources to find the main idea and supporting evidence or proofs.

  • As suggested in the video, you have to act somewhat like a detective to determine what information is important in a document and what details are irrelevant. When reading an article or journal for the first time, you must be sure to differentiate between the topic and the main idea.
  • A topic is the subject matter, or what the article is about. The main idea or thesis is the author’s view on the topic, or what they have to say about it.
  • Now that you can identify the topic and main idea, it’s time to find some supporting details. Consider the examples in the quiz again. Mouse over the highlighted areas in each example to learn its purpose.
  • It is important to remember that the topic sentence is not always found at the beginning of a paragraph; sometimes it is found in the middle or even at the end. You must be an active reader and fully digest what the author is saying.
  • If you are only reading one paragraph, it may be easy to find the topic and main idea. This becomes a bit more challenging with longer sources. It is important to remember that the process remains the same.

Research Inquiry Process

  • The third step of the research inquiry process in social science is processing information. 
  • You have already had some experience assessing and recording information. In this next part of the activity, you will learn to synthesize it.
  • One way that social scientists synthesize information is to write a summary. You have already developed some of these skills in the first part of this activity when you learned to identify the topic, main ideas, and supporting evidence.

Writing a Summary

Every discipline has unique formats for summaries. In this social science course, a summary will include:

  •  A statement about what was written (topic/research) and why (purpose).
  •  A sentence that describes the main idea/argument.
  • Two or three sentences that identify the supporting evidence or findings. Think about how the evidence connects back to the main idea/argument.
  • One sentence about how this resource would be useful for a social scientist studying the topic. Note that this will likely not be in the resource and will be based on your assessment of the information above.

When writing a summary, consider the following steps:

1. Read and highlight the resource.  You are looking for the topic, main ideas, and supportive evidence. Highlight anything that addresses bulleted points of what a summary should include.

2. Re-read your highlighted sections.  Make written, point-form notes for each part of the summary. Your notes become your outline.

3. Write your first draft.

4. Compare your draft with the checklist below. Make edits as necessary.

5. Make changes and submit when you are satisfied.

Remember:

  • Ignore minor details. Ask yourself if the information is truly relevant for people to understand the main idea.
  • Avoid using direct quotations.
  • Paraphrase; that is, put the ideas in your own words.

Level 4

The Ottawa Citizen examines a report on youth homelessness as a way to further diminish adults ending up on the street. Several agencies came together in 2015 in a group named A Way Home Ottawa to create a strategic project to intervene with youth homelessness. They created a report that took an in-depth look at various aspects that led to youth homelessness. They created the Housing First approach to provide homes for people regardless of their situation. They indicate that the best situation would be to remove the barriers that prevent the youth from leading the life to which they aspire. When there is a better understanding of what makes homeless youth different, it can be changed for the better. This article is useful for social scientists because it gives current, Canadian information on youth homelessness and possible intervention strategies.
