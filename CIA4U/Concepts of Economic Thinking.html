Concepts of Economic Thinking

"Concepts of Economic Thinking" encompass the fundamental principles that underlie the field of economics. These concepts include supply and demand, opportunity cost, scarcity, incentives, and rational decision-making, among others. Economic thinking is essential for understanding how societies allocate resources and make decisions, influencing policy, business strategies, and individual choices.

Economic Significance

Determines the importance of economic policies, decisions, practices, issues, trends, theories, and/or developments, the impact that such policies, decisions, and so on have on individuals, groups, institutions, and/or nations, and how the impact of those allocations may be different for different groups.

Sample Questions:

  1. How might a pipeline affect energy transportation from its source to markets? How does this connect to other issues/needs?
  2. How might the building of a pipeline impact the environment? Future generations?
  3. How might his pipeline affect the price of oil for consumers in the short or long run?

Cause and Effect

Determines factors that affect or contribute to economic policies, decisions, practices, trends, and/or developments, as well as the consequences of these policies, decisions, and so on, both intended and unintended. 

Sample Questions:

  1. How has our reliance on oil prompted the need/desire to build pipelines?
  2. What possible adverse effects might arise from the building and use of this pipeline?

Stability and Variability

Determine how and why economic activity and/or well-being changes or stays the same over time and investigate various factors that contribute to economic change and/or help ensure stability.

Sample Questions:

  1. How might this pipeline affect water and other resources in the future?
  2. Will more accessible transportation of oil reserves encourage continued reliance on nonrenewable energy? 
  3. What could the government do to encourage more renewable energy sources?

Economic Perspective

In economics, people may have diverse perspectives on the same policy or decision, and our economic perspective affects our personal economic/financial decisions. In addition, economic policies and decisions may benefit some groups while disadvantaging others. Economic development may have social, cultural, and environmental, as well as direct economic, effects. 

Sample Questions:

  1. How might this pipeline contribute to jobs in the local community?
  2. Why might Indigenous peoples object to building this pipeline on traditional lands?
  3. What are the benefits of this pipeline to oil producers?
