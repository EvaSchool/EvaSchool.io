Citing Your Sources

Academic Honesty

Always honestly do your best work.
Plagiarism is a specific kind of academic dishonesty that includes: Submitting a document written by someone else.
Using someone else's words without quotation marks and without giving credit to the author.
Presenting someone else's ideas (in your own words) without giving credit.

True or False

 

Why do I need to Cite a Source?

  • When the information you share is not "common knowledge," you must cite your source.
  • A "citation" is how you tell your readers that specific material in your work came from another source and how to find that source.
  • You need to cite your source when you quote (use another person's exact words in quotation marks.).
  • Moreover, when you paraphrase (use another person's ideas in your own words and sentence structure.).
  • Always ask your teacher which style, MLA or APA, you should use for citing your source and follow those rules.

In-Text Citation

Definition: In-text citations are used within the text of your paper to indicate where ideas, studies, quotes, or any other information originated from someone else's work. It's a brief reference that allows the reader to locate the source in the full References list at the end of the paper. The primary purpose of in-text citations is to avoid plagiarism, give credit to the original author, and allow the reader to verify the cited sources.

Flip

References

Definition: The References list appears at the end of the paper and provides the full citation details for all the sources cited in the text. This list allows readers to locate and retrieve each source. The References section serves to credit the original authors and provide a reliable pathway for readers to follow up on the sources used in your paper. This contributes to the academic integrity of your work and facilitates further research.

APA: References 

The References page is your list of sources at the end of your report.  

  • Start the reference list on a new page.
  • Centre the title “References” (Do not underline or italicize).
  • Alphabetize the entries.
  • Double-space all entries.
  • Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Publication date). Title of article. Title of Journal, volume number, page range. Retrieved from

http://www.someaddress.com/full/url/

An APA Citation includes:

  • The author.
  • The date published.
  • The title of the work.
  • The name and location of the publisher.
  • The page numbers.
  • For web sources, the URL.

Flip

In-Text Citation

These are citations within the body of your paper that provide brief information about the source, typically the author's last name and the page number (if applicable). Supposed you're referencing a book with the following information: Author: John Smith Title: "Economics in the Modern World" Page: Page 45 Your in-text citation for a direct quote from page 45 of this book would look like this: "According to John Smith, 'Economics is a social science that studies the production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services' (45)." In this example, the author's last name is given along with the page number in parentheses to indicate the specific source and location of the quoted material.

Works Cited

These are detailed citations listed at the end of your paper under the "Works Cited" or "Bibliography" section. They provide complete information about each source you used in your paper, allowing readers to locate the source if they wish to consult it. Book: Author: Smith, John. Title: "Economics in the Modern World." Publisher: Academic Press, 2020. In-Text Citation: (Smith 45)
