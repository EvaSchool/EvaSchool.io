The Inquiry Process

Thinking critically about local and global events, issues and leaders means economists must be able to inquire and sort through vast amounts of information from various sources. 

This process is known as the inquiry process. The Inquiry process has many components you will be expected to learn and use in this course. They include:

 

Accordion

Select each item to learn more.

Formulate Questions

Formulate questions related to the applicable overall expectation to identify their inquiry's focus.

At this stage of the inquiry process, we: 

  • Notice, wonder, and ask questions about a topic of interest.
  • Share our thinking and questions with our peers and others.
  • Dialogue about possible ways to learn more.
  • Review and re-frame our questions.
  • Make predictions about possible outcomes or answers.

Gather and Organize

Collect and organize relevant data, evidence, and/or information from primary and secondary sources and/or field studies.

At this stage of the inquiry process, we: 

  • Gather information first-hand in a range of ways and from a variety of sources
  • Connect our current thinking to previous knowledge
  • Clarify and extend our questions
  • Talk about our observations and ideas to generate more questions
  • Record information and keep work samples 

Interpret and Analyze

Analyze the data, evidence, and information using different types of graphic organizers as appropriate.

At this stage of the inquiry process, we: 

  • Compare, sort, classify, and interpret information
  • Use the information to answer our questions and test hypotheses
  • Describe characteristics and make a note of patterns
  • Talk about what we have learned and understand
  • Conclude our questions and our hypothesis
  • Create new questions and hypotheses 

Evaluate and Draw Conclusions

Synthesize data, evidence, and/or information, and make informed, critical judgments based on that data, evidence, and/or information.

At this stage of the inquiry process, we: 

  • Reflect on what we learned, as well as how and why we learned.
  • Make connections between our prior knowledge and our discoveries.
  • Apply our new understandings to different situations.
  • Ask how we have changed the way we think about other things in the world.
  • Pose new, more profound questions for an independent investigation.

Communicate

Communicate judgments, decisions, conclusions, predictions, and/or action plans clearly and logically.

At this stage of the inquiry process, we: 

  • Consider a variety of representations and plan ways to express our learning.
  • Identify different avenues for action and application.
  • Create opportunities together to celebrate our learning.

 

Quick Question

You live in a world where information is available at your fingertips at any time of the day or night. However, it is crucial to recognize that you must ask questions to pursue your curiosity to access all that information.

Within disciplines, there are often specific ways of thinking about that particular subject. In economics, for example, we are interested in asking questions that target the following:

  • Economic Significance: Why is an economic issue important?
  • Economic Perspective: How might different stakeholders view this economic controversy?
  • Cause and Effect: How do individuals, groups, and governments influence economic policies/ decisions? How are these stakeholders affected by economic policies/ decisions?
  • Stability and Variability: How do economic pressures contribute to patterns or trends, and how does economic change or steadiness influence economic decision-making
  • How does the interaction of different sectors lead to stability and change, and how do they respond to stability and/or variability over time?

A practical question in economics should:

  • generate other additional questions;
  • lead to more than one possible answer or response;
  • link to essential ideas in the discipline;
  • connect to a concept of economic thinking;
  • focus upon aspects of the content or evidence under exploration.
