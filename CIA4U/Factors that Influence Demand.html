Factors that Influence Demand

Many factors influence demand and supply. This will cause the demand curve or the supply curve to shift. Sometimes they can use both shifts at the same time.

  • Number of buyers (e.g., population size can change, and advertising can influence consumer decisions).
  • Income - demand for luxury items will increase with income:
    • Everyday goods - products whose demand changes directly with income (e.g., caviar and jewelry increase demand when income rises). If it is a normal good, then demand that good increases when income increases.
    • Inferior goods - products whose demand increases inversely with income (e.g., used clothing, ground beef). If it is an inferior good, the demand decreases when income increases.

  • Consumer preference - changes in preferences/issues influence choices such as nutrition, environment, and advertising.

  • Consumer expectations - belief about the future. E.g., if you believe the price will go down in the future, you delay the purchase, which can influence the market (not just one person, though).

  • Prices of other goods/services:

    • Substitute goods/services - you only need one or the other (e.g., butter/margarine, iPhone/Blackberry. If the price of a substitute decreases, you will buy more of the substitute good and vice-versa.

    • Complementary goods/services - goods/services consumed together, such as shoes/shoelaces, car/gas. If the price of an excellent complementary decreases, you will buy more of it and vice-versa.

It is essential to note the difference between a “change in demand” and a “change in quantity demanded.” If the ONLY factor that changes is the price, then the result would be a movement along the demand curve from one quantity to another. 

The demand curve will not shift; instead, there will be a movement along the original demand curve. In this case, we would say there was a “change in quantity demanded.” If another factor (other than price) changes, the demand curve will shift altogether, which would be identified as a “change in demand.”
