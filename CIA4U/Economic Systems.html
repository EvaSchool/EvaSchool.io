Economic Systems

   

Throughout time, economic systems have evolved to answer the problem of scarcity by focusing on the following three basic economic questions: 

  • What goods and services should be produced (and how much)?  
  • How should these goods and services be produced?
  • For whom should these goods and services be

Traditional System

A Traditional Economy answers the Three basic questions based on “The way they have always been done.” In this system, practices are passed down from generation to generation. If your father were a fisherman, you would take up his craft.

 

Advantages

  • You know what is expected of you
  • Family and community ties are usually solid    

Disadvantages

  • Change is discouraged and even punished
  • No social or economic movement
  • Methods of production are archaic and inefficient
  • Not a ton of variation in consumer goods

Command System

In a command system, individuals have little influence or choice in economic decisions.
Government Leaders make decisions as opposed to tradition.

Things that are chosen for you:

  • What product or service?
  • How are they distributed?
  • What are your career and your pay?

Advantages

  •  None   

Disadvantages

  • Complete lack of Incentives
  • Lack of consumer choices and freedoms
  • Typically comes with oppression and tyranny

Market System

Also known as Capitalism, individuals make economic decisions in a market economy for their best interests.

For the most part, it is the individuals who answer the three basic questions and thus own the Factors of Production

  • Land
  • Labor
  • Capital
  • Entrepreneurship

Mixed System

Command System + Market System

  • While private ownership and individual decisions are protected, governments intervene to regulate the markets

Laws Protecting:

  • Private property
  • Environmental Safety
  • Workers’ Rights
  • Monopoly Prevention
