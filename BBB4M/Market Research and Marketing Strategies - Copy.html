Marketing Research

   

The process of gathering, analyzing, and interpreting includes the following:

  • information about a market
  • about a product or service to be offered for sale in that market
  • about the past, present, and potential customers
  • the characteristics, spending habits, location, and needs of your business's target market
  • the industry as a whole
  • the particular competitors

   

Marketing Research Objective: Allows business owners to determine the feasibility of a business before committing substantial resources to the venture.
  
Need for market research: 

After researching, products are designed or redesigned and plans and strategies for sales are made. Market research will help:

  • reduce risks associated with new product launches
  • predict future demand changes
  • explain patterns in sales of existing products and market trends
  • assess the most favored designs, styles, promotions, and packages for a product.

1. Identify the problem

The Research Team must determine the purpose of the study to define the Research Problem. Three types of objectives can be deployed in marketing research:

    1. Exploratory research
      • Used to better define a problem or scout opportunities.
      • In-depth interviews and discussion groups are commonly used.
    2. Descriptive research
      • Used to assess a situation in the marketplace (i.e., potential for a specific product or consumer attitudes).
      • Methods include personal interviews and surveys.
    3. Casual research
      • Used for testing cause and effect relationships.
      • Typically through estimation.

2. Develop the research plan

a. Step two includes formulating an objective or theoretical framework, analytical models, research questions, and hypotheses, and identifying characteristics or factors that can influence the research design.

b. This process is guided by discussions with management and industry experts, case studies and simulations, analysis of secondary data, qualitative research, and pragmatic considerations.

3. Research design formulation

a. Research design is a framework or blueprint for conducting a marketing research project.

b. It details the procedures necessary for obtaining the required information, and its purpose is to design a study that will test the hypotheses of interest, determine possible answers to the research questions, and provide the information needed for decision-making.

c. Decisions are also made regarding what data should be obtained from the respondents (e,g, by conducting a survey or an experiment).

d. A questionnaire and sampling plan also are designed to select the most appropriate respondents for the study. 

4. Data Collection

a. In marketing research, most of the data you collect will be quantitative (numbers or data) versus qualitative, which is descriptive and observational. Ideally, you will gather a mix of the two types of data.    

      1. Primary Data
      2. Secondary Data

b. Marketing research aims to provide accurate information that reflects a true state of affairs and thus, should be conducted impartially. While research is always influenced by the researcher’s philosophy, it should be free from the personal or political biases of the researcher or the management. This is especially important in the data collection phase. The data collected will be analyzed and used to make marketing decisions. Hence, the data collection process must be free of as much bias as possible.

5. Data preparation and analysis

a. Data Analysis is an important step in the Marketing Research process where data is organized, reviewed, verified, and interpreted.

b. Analysis of data is a process of inspecting, cleaning, transforming, and modeling data to highlight useful information, suggesting conclusions, and supporting decision-making.

c. Data analysis has multiple facets and approaches, encompassing diverse techniques under a variety of names in different business, science, and social science domains.

d. Data mining is a particular data analysis technique that focuses on modeling and knowledge discovery for predictive rather than purely descriptive purposes.

e. Marketers use databases to extract applicable information that identifies customer patterns, characteristics, and behaviors.

6. Report preparation and presentation

a. During the Report Preparation & Presentation step, the entire project should be documented in a written report that addresses the specific research questions identified; describes the approach, the research design, data collection, and data analysis procedures adopted; and presents the results and the major findings. This permanent document is also helpful because it can be easily referenced by others who may not have been part of the research.

b. A successful presentation will include but is not limited to the following elements:

    • Conclusions (based on the insights gathered from data collected) that effectively meet the initial objectives of the research.

c. Recommendations about how to apply the research.

d. Charts, graphs, and visual elements that help showcase important facts and make the presentation easily digestible and memorable.
