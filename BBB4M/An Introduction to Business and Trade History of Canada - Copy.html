An Introduction to Business and Trade History of Canada

A business is defined as an organization that produces a good or offers a service in exchange for money to make a profit. To conduct business, a company completes various transactions: an exchange of things of value. A domestic business makes most of its transactions within the borders of the country in which it is based.

International Business

  • The economic system of transactions conducted between businesses located in different countries.
  • Any business that conducts financial transactions outside its native country is international.
  • Can be companies, government organizations, or non-profits.

Ways to be considered an international business

  • Export to businesses in another country. (ex. Canada Goose)
  • Import from businesses in another country. (ex. Canadian Tire)
  • Invest in businesses in another country. (ex. Fiat merger with Chrysler)
  • Own a retail or distribution outlet in another country. (ex. Amazon)
  • Own a manufacturing plant in another country. (ex. Magna)

Why TRADE?

  • To get the goods we do not have in exchange for the goods we do have.
  • Both parties expect to be better off.
  • The reliance of people on each other for goods, services, or ideas is known as interdependence.
  • When a business in Canada develops a relationship with a business in another country, that country is considered a trading partner.

Balance of TRADE?

  • The difference between a country’s exports and imports is known as the balance of trade.
  • A country that exports more goods and services than it imports has a trade surplus (it sells more than it buys).
  • A country that imports more goods and services than it exports has a trade deficit (it buys more than it sells).
    • exports > imports = trade surplus
    • exports < imports = trade deficit

International Trade

Trade with the United States

  • The United States declared independence from Britain in the late 1700s.
  • Needed to become self-reliant.
  • The invention of the steam engine and cotton gin helped the rapid growth of American industry.
  • Canada supplied raw materials that were needed in the United States.
  • The United States became Canada's largest trading partner—this remains true today.

Trade with Mexico

  • Developed since signing NAFTA in 1993.
  • Goods made in Mexico and the United States now enter Canada duty-free.
  • Mexico has become one of Canada's top five trading partners since 2000.

Trade with the Middle East

  • Has traditionally centered on oil, but this commodity is not sustainable.
  • Political instability and lack of industrialization in much of the region have limited trade.
  • United Arab Emirates (Dubai), Israel, and Egypt have established trading relationships with Canadian businesses that do not depend on oil.

Trade with India

  • Population of over one billion people.
  • The workforce is generally young and well-educated.
  • Has become a major center of outsourcing and manufacturing.    
  • Lack of infrastructure and widespread corruption are persistent problems.
  • Indian companies are aggressively expanding into international markets.

Trade with Africa

  • African imports to Canada are very low.
  • Business opportunities are limited by unstable governments, lack of infrastructure, and rural economies.
  • Rich in primary resources.
  • Some countries (Morocco, and South Africa) are beginning to emerge as major trading partners.
