International Business

International business refers to the trade of goods and services, capital, knowledge, and technology across borders on a global scale. International business transactions include contractual agreements that permit foreign firms to utilize services, products, and processes from different countries.

International strategy:

An effective international strategy prioritizes a concentrated point of operation when exporting products and services globally. Consequently, it places a lower emphasis on both global integration and local responsiveness.

Multidomestic strategy:

A multi-domestic strategy places a significant emphasis on local responsiveness and a lower emphasis on global integration, making it the foremost "local-first" approach among the four strategies. Companies that adopt a multi-domestic strategy adapt their products, messaging, go-to-market strategies, and customer support (among other factors) according to the unique characteristics and demands of each market they enter.

Global strategy: 

On the opposite end of the global integration/local responsiveness spectrum lies the adoption of a global strategy. This approach prioritizes standardization across various aspects such as colors, messaging, products, and operations, enabling companies to establish consistent and scalable processes regardless of the foreign market they operate. This entails maintaining a single brand, a unified suite of products, and a cohesive message originating from a central headquarters.

 Transnational strategy:

Transnational corporations maintain a central or head office in one country, emphasizing global integration, while also establishing local subsidiaries in international markets to enhance local responsiveness. By adopting this approach, they benefit from a combination of advantages: a unified brand that provides an organized and efficient center of operations, coupled with the ability to adapt to local market preferences and tastes as required.

Trade Agreements

It is an enforceable treaty between two or more countries that addresses the movement of goods and services, eliminates trade barriers, establishes terms of trade, and encourages foreign investment. 

Examples: 

  • NAFTA
  • Canada-Korean Free Trade Agreement (CKFTA)
  • Canada-European Union Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement (CETA)

Tax Agreements

  • A tax treaty was created to prevent tax evasion and to prevent double taxation for people who would pay taxes in Canada and another country on the same income.
  • Determines how much tax each country can collect on income received from pensions, wages, salaries, or interest.
  • Canada has tax treaties with 92 countries, including the United States, Japan, India, and New Zealand, and continues to negotiate and re-negotiate treaties with other countries.

European Sovereign Debt Crisis

  • The European sovereign debt crisis was a period when several European countries experienced the collapse of financial institutions, high government debt, and rapidly rising bond yield spreads in government securities.
  • The European sovereign debt crisis began in 2008 with the collapse of Iceland's banking system.
  • Some of the contributing causes included the financial crisis of 2007 to 2008, and the Great Recession of 2008 through 2012.
  • The crisis peaked between 2010 and 2012.

The British exit or Brexit 

  • In June 2016, the United Kingdom voted to leave the European Union in a referendum. This vote fueled Eurosceptics across the continent, and speculation soared that other countries would leave the EU. After a drawn-out negotiation process, Brexit took place at 11 pm Greenwich Mean Time, Jan. 31, 2020, and did not precipitate any groundswell of sentiment in other countries to depart the EMU.
  • It's a common perception that this movement grew during the debt crisis, and campaigns have described the EU as a "sinking ship."

Canada and EU Free Trade Agreement: CETA

  • The agreement was made in principle, but individual ratifications are still in process as of 2018.
  • CETA is a new trade agreement between the EU and Canada.
  • It cuts tariffs and makes it easier to export goods and services, benefitting people and businesses in both the EU and Canada.
  • CETA entered into force provisionally on 21 September 2017, meaning most of the agreement now applies.

The Role of the United Nations in International Business

The United Nations is an international organization founded in 1945 after the Second World War by 51 countries committed to maintaining international peace and security, developing friendly relations among nations, and promoting social progress, better living standards, and human rights.

Five main purposes of the United Nations:

  • Maintain international peace and security.
  • Protect human rights.
  • Deliver humanitarian aid.
  • Promote sustainable development.
  • Uphold international law.

 

The Paris Agreement

  • On December 12, 2015, the Paris Agreement, came into effect as the largest international climate agreement to date. 
  • The Paris Agreement's central aim is to strengthen the global response to the threat of climate change by keeping a global temperature rise this century well below 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit the temperature increase even further to 1.5 degrees Celsius.

UN as a Business Partner 

  • Global businesses sell to the UN just as they do to their own governments and public-sector organizations. Each arm of the UN has a procurement office. The UN Procurement Division does business with vendors from all over the world and is actively working to increase its sources of supply from developing countries and countries with economies in transition.
