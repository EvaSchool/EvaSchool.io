Cornell Note-Taking Method

 

Guidelines for Taking Good Notes

This is an important skill to have for post-secondary education - but it can be tricky!

              

Some Recommended Guidelines for Note-Taking
  1. Concentrate on the lecture or on the reading material.
  2. Take notes consistently.
  3. Take notes selectively. Do not try to write down every word. Remember that the average lecturer speaks approximately 125-140 words per minute, and the average note-taker writes at a rate of about 25 words per minute.
  4. Translate ideas into your own words.
  5. Organize notes into some sort of logical form.
  6. Be brief. Write down only the major points and important information.
  7. Write legibly. Notes are useless if you cannot read them later!
  8. Ensure you write your notes accurately so you can easily understand them when you refer to them later.

   

Why Not Just Record the Lecture?

This may be a feasible option if you are an auditory learner, but it’s still important to go back and summarize the key points in written form.

  

Problems With Solely Recording Lectures  Some Problems With Recording Lectures

Many students come to class with their devices, press record, and tune out, thinking "I'll listen to it later."

  • On the surface, this seems like a good approach.
  • With the lecture recorded you have a permanent record of the presentation, but this is not an effective approach for several reasons.
  • Instead of actively listening and making good notes during the original lecture, the same work has to be done at a later time, so this approach takes too much time.
  • Notes are a condensed and distilled version of the lecture's important points. It takes five minutes to read over good notes, in which time you can highlight what are the really important parts, but it takes an hour or more to re-listen to a lecture, containing much unnecessary information.
  • It is the actual process of note-taking, physically writing/typing points down that helps you learn.

  

Introduction to Cornell Note-Taking

The Cornell Method of taking notes is both effective and popular. This strategy is designed to help you get your notes right the first time around (during and just after the class) so you don't have to go through and copy out legible study notes a second time. The idea is to have one–shot, effective, study–worthy lecture notes.

The 6 Rs of This Method:

Record
Reduce

Recite
Reflect

Review
Recapitulate

  

Open All Panels

Record

  • Record (write) the lecture in the Notes section
  • Pay attention to main ideas and supporting facts.
  • Eliminate unnecessary words, and don't worry about spelling and grammar as long as you can still read and understand your notes after!

Reduce

  • As soon as possible, summarize these ideas and facts concisely in the Recall Column.
  • Summarizing clarifies meanings and relationships, reinforces continuity, and strengthens memory.
  • Also, this refining allows you to prepare for exams gradually and well ahead of time.

Recite

  • Now cover the Notes section and use only your jottings in the Cues Column as "flags" to help you recall.
  • Recite the facts and ideas of the lecture as fully as you can in your own words.
  • Then, uncovering your notes, verify what you have said.
  • This procedure helps you transfer the facts and ideas into your long-term memory.

Reflect

  • Reflective students distill their opinions from their notes by thinking about the points they have included in the note-taking.
  • Such reviewing helps students make sense of their courses and lectures because in doing so, students see relationships among ideas and concepts.

Review

  • If you spend 10 minutes every week or so in a quick review of these notes, you will retain most of what you have learned, and you will be able to use your knowledge currently to greater and greater effectiveness.

Recapitulate

  • Recapitulate means to rephrase or state again.
  • This is the point at which you can put the notes away completely and are able to state the main points of a lecture with no cues - you now own the information - it is part of you, and you can use it.

  

Using the Template Notes May Differ!

You can easily develop a Cornell Notes template in Word or PDF on your computer, but I will share a Word document that you can save and use.

In the end, you don't need a template – drawing a few lines on a piece of paper before a lecture sets you up just the same.

Your notes may differ from those I’ve provided, or those from your peers - as everyone has a unique style of learning.

You may notice that the “notes” we’ve given seem sparse because we’ve included only key points and a few cues, but they allow us to complete the rest of the page and flesh out the ideas further.
