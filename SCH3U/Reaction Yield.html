In practice, however, theoretical yields are rarely achieved. Instead, the actual yield is usually less.

Materials are lost through:

  • spillage during the transfer of solutions
  • splattering during heating
  • losses during the isolation and purification of the product.

Chemists can reduce these losses by:

  • improving their skills
  • using better equipment
  • reducing the number of steps in the procedure

The mass of the reactant in any sample of the starting chemical is therefore less than the measured mass. Failure to take into account the impurities when determining the theoretical yield results in a predicted amount of product that is impossible to achieve. As chemicals age, they may become more impure—particularly if they are not stored properly.

During the synthesis of cisplatin, the chemotherapy drug introduced in Section 7.3, a similar compound called transplatin is also formed.

Cisplatin is an important drug in the treatment of cancer. The yield of cisplatin is reduced by a competing reaction that produces transplatin.

The ratio, expressed as a percentage, of the actual yield to the theoretical yield

It can be calculated either by comparing the actual mass of the product with the theoretical mass, or by comparing the actual amount of the product with the theoretical amount.

Callout

Determining Percentage Yield

Use stoichiometry (based on the balanced chemical equation) to determine the theoretical yield of the desired product.

If you are told which reactant limiting reagent is, use its mass to calculate theoretical yield. If the masses of both reactants are given, you must first determine the limiting reagent.

Make that the yield theoretical yield is expressed in the same units: either grams or moles. Note that percentage yield has no units because the units of actual and theoretical yield cancel each other in the calculation.
