Electric Fields

The Electric Field

The region in which a force is exerted on an electric charge; the electric force per unit positive charge; the unit is N/C.

In general, charges an electric field around it, through which the electric force is transmitted on other electric charges. 
Electric Field is a vector quantity which means that it has both magnitude and direction. 
The electric field is measured in N/C.
The electric field is represented by what is called “ The Electric Field Lines”.

The Electric Field Line

It is an imaginary line through a region of space so that its tangent at any point is in the direction of the electric field vector at this point. 

The electric field and its properties can be represented by these lines.  The direction of the electric field is parallel to these lines. If the charge is positive, the electric field lines are directed outward, away from the charge.

If the charge is negative, the electric field lines are directed inward, toward the charge.

Properties of the Electric Field

The magnitude of the electric field is proportional to the density of the field lines.  The density of the field lines decreases further away from the charge.
Therefore, the strengths of the electric field and the electric force decrease as r^2 increases.

The density of the electric field lines increases as the quantity of charge q increases.

Therefore, the strengths of the electric field and the electric force increase as q increases.

Electric Dipole

A pair of equal and opposite electric charges with centers separated by a small distance.

The electric field from both charges merges at a point midway between the charges. The dipole produces a net electric field. The direction of this electric field always points from the positive charge to the negative charge, and it is almost parallel to the line connecting the two charges.

E decreases at distances that are further from the dipole.
Notice that the electric field lines are symmetric around the vertical axis.

Dipole-Like Arrangement

By considering the case of a Dipole-Like arrangement of two charges that have different magnitudes and signs, we can see that:

The electric field lines are not symmetric around the vertical axis, midway between the two charges. 

Half of the electric field lines from the positive charge converge on the negative charge.  At a large distance, where r is much greater separation, the electric field radiates outward.

Two alike Charges separated by a small distance

In the case of two alike charges that are equal, the electric field lines extend outward from both charges.

These field lines don’t connect at any point, producing a disk-shaped region of zero electric field everywhere around the midpoint between the two charges. 

The field lines appear to be radiating from a single-point charge.

Uniform Electric Field

For the case of two parallel planes of uniformly distributed charge with one plane having a positive charge and the other having a negative charge, we can see that the electric field extends from the positive plane to the negative side. 

The electric field lines are straight, parallel to each other, and perpendicular to the plane. The electric field is uniformly distributed between the plates as long as the separation between the plates is much smaller than their surface area.

Thunderstorms

  

Earth is bombarded with higher energetic particles coming from outer space, causing the ionosphere to have a positive charge. On the other hand, Earth’s surface is more negatively charged. A permanent electric field exists throughout the atmosphere. The greatest and most sudden change to the electric field occurs during thunderstorms. Cloud-to-ground lightning accompanies these storms.

Electric Fields and Animals

Hammerhead sharks detect electric fields generated by muscle contractions in goby. 

Although the shark cannot see the goby, it can detect these electric fields up to 25 cm above the sand.

Air Purification and Electrostatic Precipitators

Electrostatic precipitators use electric fields to remove extremely small particles of soot, dust, and ash from flue gases.

Once the flue gas and the particles it contains pass between the wire and the plate, the wire transfers electrons to the small particles, making particles negative.  The electric field separates the small particles from the flue gas.
