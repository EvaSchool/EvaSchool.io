Forces and Free-body Diagrams

Force ()

We define force to be a push or a pull.
The SI unit for force is Newton (N)

You can simply apply a force to a box by either pushing  or pulling it. 
You can notice that there is a direction and a magnitude for the force applied, that’s why force is a vector quantity.

  • Types of Forces

    In Physics, we classify forces in two different types; Non-Contact Forces, and Contact Forces. 
    Non-Contact Forces: Forces which occur between two objects that are not touching each other (require no Physical contact between the objects).
    Contact Forces: Forces which occurs between two objects that are touching each other (require a Physical contact between the objects).

Examples on Non-Contact Forces

There are forces which does not require a physical contact between the object like

Force of gravity ( ) : All objects which have mass attract each other due to this force. The earth pulls down the apple due to gravity!!

Magnetic force: Two magnets repel or attract each other due to this force.

 Normal Force  

This is a force perpendicular to the surface between objects in contact.

When you sit on a chair, the force of gravity (your weight) pulls you down. But, Have you ever wondered why you are not falling down?! This because of the normal force, which acts upward and balances with the force of gravity.

Tension  

It is a pulling force exerted by objects such as strings and ropes.

If you hanged a certain mass to a rope as shown in the figure, the rope will exert a pulling force up to the mass, and at the same time the mass will exert a pulling force on your hand down. 
The force of tension is the same along the length of the rope.

Friction  

A force that opposes the sliding of two surfaces across one another; acts opposite to motion or attempted motion.

Static Friction  

a force that resists attempted motion between two surfaces in contact

Kinetic Friction  

a force exerted on a moving object by a surface in the direction of motion opposite to the motion of the object.

Air resistance  

The Force due to friction between objects and the air around them.

For example, as you ride a bicycle, the air will always resist the direction of motion due to friction between the bicycle and the air surrounding it. 

Applied Force  

A force due to one object pushing or pulling on another

In your daily life, you apply forces in a form of a push or pull to different object. This force is considered as a contact force, as it requires a contact between you and that object.
