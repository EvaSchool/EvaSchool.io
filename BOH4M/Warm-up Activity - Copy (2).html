Warm-up Activity

Callout

Some 40 climbers are winding their way to the top of Mt. Everest. About 1,000 feet below the summit sits a British mountain climber in trouble, collapsed in a shallow snow cave. Most of those on the way up just look while continuing their climbs. Sherpas from one passing team pause to give him oxygen before moving on. Within hours, David Sharp, 34, is dead of oxygen deficiency on the mountain. A climber who passed by says: “At 28,000 feet it's hard to stay alive yourself. he was in very poor condition ..., it was a very hard decision . . . he wasn’t a member of our team.” Someone who made the summit in the past says: “If you're going to go to Everest . . . | think you have to accept responsibility that you may end up doing something that's not ethically nice... you have to realize that you're in a different world.” After hearing about this case, the late Sir Edmund Hillary, who reached the top in 1953, said: “Human life is far more important than just getting to the top of a mountain.”

 

Multi-Select

Ethical Dilemma: Climbers on Mt. Everest encounter a situation where a fellow climber, David Sharp, is in distress and in need of assistance while they are on their way to the summit. They must decide whether to prioritize reaching the summit, considering the extreme conditions at high altitudes, or to provide assistance to the distressed climber.
