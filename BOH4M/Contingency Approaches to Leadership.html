Contingency Approaches to Leadership

 

Introduction

As leadership research progressed, scholars began questioning the notion that a "high-high" leader was always the most effective. They realized the need to explore when and under what circumstances specific leadership styles are preferable. This led to the development of contingency approaches aimed at understanding the conditions for leadership success in various situations.

Fiedler's Contingency Model

Understanding Leadership Style

Fred Fiedler's model asserts that effective leadership depends on aligning leadership style with situational demands. Leadership style is measured using the least-preferred co-worker (LPC) scale, reflecting either task-motivated (low LPC) or relationship-motivated (high LPC) tendencies. Fiedler believes that leadership style is a durable aspect of one's personality.

Understanding Leadership Situations

The amount of control a situation allows the leader is crucial in determining the correct style-situation fit. Three contingency variables diagnose situational control: leader-member relations, task structure, and position power. These variables result in eight leadership situations, ranging from highly favorable to unfavorable.

Matching Leadership Style and Situation

 

In Fiedler's research, neither a task-oriented nor a relationship-oriented leadership style proved universally effective. Instead, each style worked best in specific situations. Two propositions summarize Fiedler's findings:

Proposition 1: A task-oriented leader is most successful in highly favorable or highly unfavorable situations.

Proposition 2: A relationship-oriented leader is most successful in moderately favorable situations.

Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership Model

Contrary to Fiedler's view that leadership style is hard to change, the Hersey-Blanchard model suggests that successful leaders adjust their styles based on follower maturity. "Readiness" reflects followers' ability and willingness to perform tasks in a given situation. Four leadership styles result from combining task-oriented and relationship-oriented behaviors.

Delegating

Participating

Description: Emphasizing shared ideas and participative decisions (low task, high relationship).

Selling

 Description: Explaining task directions in a supportive and persuasive way (high task, high relationship). 

Telling

Description: Giving specific task directions and closely supervising work (high task, low relationship).

The choice of style depends on follower readiness, with delegating, selling, participating, and telling being suitable for different readiness levels.

Path-Goal Leadership Theory

The path-goal theory, by Robert House, posits that an effective leader clarifies paths to help followers achieve task-related and personal goals. Leaders facilitate goal achievement by removing barriers and offering rewards. The theory recommends leaders adapt their styles to create positive path-goal linkages.

Path-Goal Contingencies

The path-goal theory advises managers to use leadership styles that match situational needs. Contingencies include follower characteristics (ability, experience, locus of control) and work environment characteristics (task structure, authority system, work group). The theory also identifies substitutes for leadership, which are aspects of the situation or people involved that make external leadership unnecessary.

Leader-Member Exchange Theory

In group settings, leaders often develop "special" relationships with certain team members. Leader-Member Exchange (LMX) theory explores these unique leader-follower relationships.

Leader-Participation Model

The Vroom-Jago Leader-Participation model suggests that leadership success depends on selecting the most appropriate decision-making method based on the problem at hand. Decision-making options include authority, consultative, and group decisions, with the choice guided by decision quality, acceptance, and time constraints.

Contingency approaches to leadership acknowledge that effective leadership is context-dependent. Leaders must adapt their styles and decision-making methods to match the situation and the readiness of their followers, ultimately contributing to the success of their teams and organizations.
