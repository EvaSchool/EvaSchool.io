What Is Ethical Behaviour?

Ethical behavior is crucial in any society or organization. It is defined as the code of moral principles that guide one's conduct. These principles help individuals distinguish between right and wrong actions within the context of accepted moral codes.

Ethical issues often arise when individuals face actions that challenge their personal beliefs.
The ethical test extends beyond legality, considering personal values, which vary among individuals.

Terminal and Instrumental Values

Terminal values are preferences about desired ends in life, such as self-respect, family security, and happiness.
Instrumental values are preferences regarding the means for accomplishing these ends, like honesty and courage.

Utilitarian View

This perspective assesses ethical behavior based on the greatest good for the greatest number.
It focuses on the consequences of actions and is results-oriented.
For example, business decision-makers often consider profits and efficiency as key criteria for ethical decisions.

Individualism View

The individualist view of ethical behavior emphasizes the long-term advancement of self-interests.
It promotes honesty and integrity to serve one's long-term interests.
Lying and cheating for short-term gain should not be tolerated.

Moral Rights View

Ethical behavior under this view respects and protects the fundamental rights of people.
It includes rights to privacy, due process, free speech, free consent, health and safety, and freedom of conscience.
The Universal Declaration of Human Rights by the United Nations aligns with this perspective.

Justice View

The justice view emphasizes fairness and equitable treatment in ethical behavior.
It considers equal distribution of benefits and burdens.
Ethical standards should be universally applied across cultures and national boundaries.

Cultural Issues in Ethical Behaviour

  • Cultural issues play a significant role in ethical behavior.
  • Cultural relativism suggests that ethical behavior is determined by cultural context.
  • Universalism, on the other hand, argues for universal ethical standards irrespective of cultural differences.

Cultural Relativism vs. Universalism

"Cultural relativism argues 'when in Rome, do as the Romans do,' while universalism suggests that ethical standards should apply consistently across all cultures and boundaries."

 

The United Nations and Human Rights

The United Nations supports the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, emphasizing the importance of human rights on a global scale.
