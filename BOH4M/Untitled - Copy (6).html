Functional Structures Divisional Structures Matrix Structures

   

Functional structures group individuals with similar skills and tasks together into formal work units. In this arrangement, each function works within its area of expertise. It is commonly used in smaller organizations dealing with a limited range of products or services.

Functional structures promote economies of scale, efficient resource use, and in-depth skill development.

However, they can suffer from problems like difficulty in pinpointing responsibilities and communication challenges between functions.

 

Divisional structures group people who work on the same product, serve similar customers or are located in the same area or region. These structures are common in complex organizations with diverse operations.

There are different types of divisional structures, including product, geographical, customer, and process structures.

Each type has its advantages, such as improved responsiveness to market demands and a clear focus on specific customers or regions. However, divisional structures can also increase complexity.

Matrix structures combine elements of functional and divisional structures by using permanent teams that cut across functions to support specific products, projects, or programs.

Workers in a matrix structure belong to both functional groups and product/project teams, reporting to two bosses.

Matrix structures offer better cooperation across functions, improved decision-making, and flexibility. However, they can also lead to power struggles and task confusion.
