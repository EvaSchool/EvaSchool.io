Follett’s Organizations as Communities

Select to learn more.

Mary Parker Follett's work marked an important transition from classical thinking to behavioral management. She described organizations as "communities" where managers and workers should collaborate harmoniously. They should resolve conflicts and differences openly and genuinely. Follett believed it was the manager's responsibility to foster cooperation and integrate interests. She proposed that making every employee an owner of a business would create a sense of collective responsibility.

The Hawthorne Studies

 The Hawthorne studies applied a scientific management perspective to understand the impact of economic incentives and workplace conditions on worker output. Elton Mayo led a team to investigate the effect of worker fatigue on productivity. Mayo's research extended to examining employee attitudes, interpersonal relations, and group dynamics. The studies, which began during the 1920s, concluded in 1932. They revealed that the same factors, such as work conditions or wages, could be sources of satisfaction for some workers and dissatisfaction for others.

Maslow’s Theory of Human Needs

Select to learn more.

Abraham Maslow's theory revolves around human needs, describing them as physiological or psychological deficiencies that individuals seek to satisfy. These needs can influence a person's work attitudes and behaviors. Maslow's theory is based on two principles: the deficit principle (satisfied needs do not motivate behavior) and the progression principle (needs exist in a hierarchy).

McGregor’s Theory X and Theory Y Douglas McGregor proposed two contrasting views:

  • Theory X assumes people dislike work, lack ambition, act irresponsibly, and prefer to be led.
  • Theory Y assumes people are willing to work, like responsibility, and are self-directed and creative. McGregor believed that managers holding these assumptions could create self-fulfilling prophecies based on their behavior.

Argyris’s Theory of Adult Personality

Select to learn more.

Chris Argyris compared management practices in traditional hierarchical organizations with the needs and capabilities of mature adults. He argued that common workplace issues like absenteeism, turnover, and low morale could indicate a mismatch. Argyris believed that managers treating people positively and as responsible adults would achieve higher productivity.
