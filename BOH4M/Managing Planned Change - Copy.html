Phases of Planned Change

Managers seeking to lead change in organizations can benefit from a simple but helpful model developed many years ago by the psychologist Kurt Lewin. He recommends that any planned change be viewed as a process with three phases.

Phase 1: Unfreezing

Unfreezing is the first phase in the planned change process. It involves preparing a system for change and developing felt needs for change. The goal is to get people to view change as a way of solving a problem or pursuing an opportunity. Some call this phase the "burning bridge" phase of change.

 

Callout

Unfreezing is like setting the stage for change. It's about making people aware of the need for change and creating a sense of urgency.

In this phase, managers can simulate the burning bridge by engaging people with facts and information that communicate the need for change, such as environmental pressures, declining performance, and examples of benchmarks or alternative approaches.

Phase 2: Changing

The changing phase follows unfreezing. This is where actual changes are made in organizational targets like tasks, people, culture, technology, and structure. It's crucial to ensure that people are prepared and feel a need for change before implementing it.

Callout

The changing phase is like building a house; a solid foundation is necessary for successful implementation.

If change is implemented prematurely, without adequate preparation and buy-in, it's more likely to fail.

Phase 3: Refreezing

The final phase is refreezing, where the manager focuses on stabilizing the change. This involves linking change with appropriate rewards, positive reinforcement, and resource support. It's essential to evaluate results, provide feedback, and make necessary modifications.

Callout

Refreezing ensures that the change becomes ingrained in normal routines and isn't easily forgotten.

The most common error at this stage is declaring victory too soon and withdrawing support before the change is fully integrated.

Improvising

In today's fast-paced environments, there's often a need for an additional phase called improvising. This phase involves making adjustments as needed while change is taking place. It's like fine-tuning the change process based on ongoing feedback.

 

Callout

Improvising is about being flexible and responsive to unforeseen challenges during the change journey.

For example, when introducing new technology, continuous feedback and improvisation may be necessary to customize it to users' needs.

Change Strategies

When it comes to implementing planned change, managers have several strategies at their disposal. Let's explore three common ones:

Force-Coercion Strategies

Force-coercion relies on formal authority, rewards, and punishments to induce change. It's often used to break old patterns and gain willingness to try new ones.

 

Callout

Force-coercion may produce temporary compliance, but it's not a long-term solution.

 

Select the item below to learn more.

Rational Persuasion Strategies

Rational persuasion relies on special knowledge, empirical data, and rational arguments to bring about change. It assumes that people are inherently rational and guided by reason.

 

Callout

Rational persuasion seeks to convince individuals that the change will leave them better off.

 

Select the item below to learn more.

Shared Power Strategies

Shared power involves collaboration and empowerment-based approaches. It's highly participative and aims to develop consensus to support needed change.

Callout

Shared power unlocks the creativity, experience, and energies of people within the system.

While it may be slow and time-consuming, it often results in longer-lasting, internalized change.

 

Select the item below to learn more.

Resistance to Change

When people resist change, they're often defending something important to them that appears threatened. It's essential for change leaders to listen to resistance and use it as a resource for improving the change process.

 

Callout

Resistance can provide valuable insights into what needs to be addressed in the change plan.

Understanding and addressing resistance is a critical aspect of successful change management.
