Competitive advantage is a crucial goal for organizations. It involves the ability to outperform rivals through effective strategies.

Typical sources of competitive advantage include:

Cost and Quality: Emphasis on operating efficiency and product/service quality.

Knowledge and Speed: Focus on innovation and rapid delivery of new ideas.

Barriers to Entry: Creating market strongholds protected from competition.

Financial Resources: Investments or loss absorption that competitors can't match.

The business-level strategy focuses on a single business unit or product line. It answers the question, "How will we compete in this industry and market?"

Functional strategy guides resource use within specific functional areas like marketing, manufacturing, finance, or human resources.

A strategy is a comprehensive action plan that identifies the long-term direction of an organization, aiming for sustainable competitive advantage. It ensures that resources are used with consistent strategic intent.

For example, Coca-Cola's strategic intent is "to put a Coke within 'arm's reach' of every consumer in the world." This guides their investments and focus. Three levels of strategy guide most enterprises: corporate strategy, business strategy, and functional strategy.

Corporate-level strategy directs the entire organization toward sustainable competitive advantage. It defines the scope of operations, answering, "In what industries and markets should we compete?"

For example, General Electric (GE) owns a wide range of businesses. Corporate-level decisions for GE involve acquisitions, expansions, and cutbacks across its diverse portfolio.
