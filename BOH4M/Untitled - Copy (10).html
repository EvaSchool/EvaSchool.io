The Communication Process

Communication is an interpersonal process of sending and receiving symbols with attached messages. One way to view the communication process is by asking a series of questions: Who? (sender), Says what? (message), In what way? (channel), To whom? (receiver), With what result?

The Key Elements


The communication process involves several key elements, as shown in Figure 9.12. These elements include:

Sender: Responsible for encoding a message into meaningful symbols, both verbal and nonverbal.

Message: The content intended to be communicated.

Communication Channel: The medium through which the message is transmitted.

Receiver: The individual who decodes or interprets the message to understand its meaning.

  

Callout

Poor Choice of Channels


A communication channel serves as the pathway or medium through which a message travels from the sender to the receiver. Effective managers choose the appropriate communication channel, or combination of channels, to achieve their intended purpose.

Written channels (paper or electronic) are suitable for simple messages and wide dissemination.
Spoken channels work best for complex messages requiring immediate feedback.

Effective Communication


Effective communication is achieved when the sender's message is fully understood by the receiver. It allows managers to establish relationships, listen to others, resolve conflicts, negotiate, and gather information for a high-performance workplace. Efficiency in communication is also vital, ensuring minimal resource expenditure.

Communication Barriers


Numerous factors can create barriers to effective communication. Some common sources of noise and interference include:

  • Poor choice of channels
  • Poor written or oral expression
  • Failure to recognize nonverbal signals
  • Physical distractions
  • Status effects

Noise, as indicated in Figure 9.12, refers to anything that hampers the effectiveness of the communication process. Foreign language situations, like the one experienced by Yoshihiro Wada, reveal the potential for noise in communication.

Poor Written or Oral Expression


Effective communication hinges on expressing messages clearly. It involves choosing words carefully to convey intentions accurately. Consider the following examples of bafflegab in executive communications:

"Consumer elements are continuing to stress the fundamental necessity of a stabilization of the price structure at a lower level than exists at the present time." (Translation: consumers want lower prices)


"Substantial economies were affected in this division by increasing the time interval between distributions of data-eliciting forms to business entities." (Translation: the division saved money by sending out fewer questionnaires)


Achieving clear expression is essential for emails, telephone calls, face-to-face meetings, formal briefings, and video conferences.

  

Callout

Failure to Recognize Nonverbal Signals

Nonverbal communication encompasses hand movements, facial expressions, body posture, eye contact, and interpersonal space. It can significantly impact message delivery, with up to 55 percent of a message's impact attributed to nonverbal communication.

Mixed messages occur when verbal and nonverbal cues convey different meanings.

  

Callout

Physical Distractions


Several physical distractions can disrupt effective communication, such as telephone interruptions, drop-in visitors, and a lack of privacy.

  

Callout

Status Effects


Filtering involves intentionally distorting information to make it appear favorable to the recipient, often by telling superiors what they want to hear. This can result in biased and inaccurate information, leading to poor decision-making.

Effective communication requires careful consideration of these elements and an awareness of potential barriers.
