Summary of Essay Types

 

Accordion

Select each item to learn more.

The Descriptive Essay

Functions:

  • provide a detailed and vivid description of a particular person, place, object, or event
  • aims to create a sensory experience for the reader by using language that appeals to the five senses: sight, sound, smell, taste, and touch

Characteristics:

  • conveys a clear and evocative image by using descriptive language, sensory details, and figurative language such as similes, metaphors, and personification
  • evokes emotions or creates a particular mood by carefully selecting and arranging words
  • capturing the beauty, uniqueness, or significance of the subject, allowing the reader to develop a greater understanding and appreciation for it

The Narrative Essay

Functions:

  • tell a story or recount a series of events in a coherent and engaging manner
  • revolves around a central theme or experience and presents it in a narrative format
  • to entertain, engage, and captivate the reader by sharing a personal or fictional story

Characteristics:

  • Often uses first-person point of view to bring a personal touch and authenticity to the storytelling
    -allows the reader to step into the writer's shoes, experiencing the events and emotions firsthand
  • may be based on real-life experiences, imaginative scenarios, or a combination of both. 

The Cause and Effect Essay

Functions:

  • to examine the relationship between events or phenomena and explain how one event (the cause) leads to another event or series of events (the effects)
  • aims to analyze and elucidate the causes and consequences of a particular situation
  • seeks to answer the question "why?" and establish the causal links between different elements

Characteristics:

  • may focus on either the causes or the effects, depending on the essay's purpose and scope
  • may discuss the effects of a particular cause, exploring the outcomes and consequences that arise as a result.

The Compare and Contrast Essay

Functions:

  • clarify something unknown or no well understood
  • leads to a fresh insight or new way of viewing something
  • brings one or both of the subjects into share focus
  • shows that one subject is better than the other

Characteristics: 

  • A comparison essay notes either similarities only, or a combination of similarities and differences
  • A contrast essay notes only differences
  • The thesis can present the subjects and indicate that they will be compared contrasted or both
  • The same points should be discussed for both subjects. 

The Process Essay

Functions:

  • to explain and instruct the reader on how to perform a particular task or process
  • provides a step-by-step guide, presenting a clear and organized sequence of actions that need to be followed to achieve a desired outcome

Characteristics:

  • breaks down a complex procedure into manageable steps, making it easier for the reader to understand and replicate
  • typically begins with an introduction that introduces the process and provides any necessary background information
  • can cover a wide range of topics and activities, including practical tasks, technical processes, cooking recipes, DIY projects, academic procedures, and more

The Expository Essay

Functions: 

  • to present, explain, and analyze a specific topic or idea in a clear and objective manner
  • to provide information, educate the reader, and explore a subject in depth

Characteristics: 

  • focuses on presenting facts, evidence, and logical explanations
  • seeks to inform the reader about a particular topic, concept, or phenomenon, offering a balanced and unbiased perspective
  • writer must conduct thorough research, gather reliable sources, and present information in a logical and organized manner

The Persuasive/Argumentative Essay

Functions: 

  • to convince the reader to adopt a particular viewpoint or take a specific course of action
  • seeks to persuade the reader and influence their beliefs, attitudes, or behaviors

 

Characteristics: 

  • writer presents a clear and compelling argument, supported by evidence, reasoning, and persuasive techniques
  • writer takes a stance on a controversial or debatable issue and uses persuasive strategies to sway the reader's opinion in favor of their viewpoint.
