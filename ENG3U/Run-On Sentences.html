Run-On Sentences

What is wrong with these sentences?

  • The heart wants what it wants it wants you.
  • You are my sunshine, you make me happy when skies are grey.

Run-On Sentences

  • This is formed when two independent clauses are joined incorrectly.
  • There are two types of run-on sentences:
i. Fused sentences

The heart wants what it wants it wants you.
ii. Comma Splice

You are my sunshine, you make me happy when skies are grey.

Fused Sentences

These are clauses joined directly:

  • no punctuation marks
  • no conjunctions.

Example:

He went home only to find out she left him without saying goodbye.

I ate dinner and had chocolate cake for dessert and then went for a walk with my dog.

Comma Splice

These clauses are joined only by using a comma, but are not completely clear and fluent.

Example:

I have died everyday waiting for you, darling don’t be afraid I have loved you.

I have a new job, it pays more than my old job.

 

Callout

CORRECTING RUN-ONS

There are different ways to correct these errors. 

Example:

Let’s go through them using the sample sentence:

I wanted him to stay, he wanted to let go.

Use a comma and a coordinating conjunction (like FANBOYS - and, but, yet, so, or, nor, for).

  • I wanted him to stay, but he wanted to let go.

Use a semicolon.

You can use a semicolon alone or with a transition word (e.g., however, at any rate, in contrast, as a result, etc.)

  • I wanted him to stay; he wanted to let go.

Put a period between them, and start the second sentence with a capital letter.

  • I wanted him to stay. He wanted to let go.

Callout

For longer run-on sentences like this one…:

“Have you ever seen the Liberty Bell it's big, it's old, and it's cracked. The Liberty Bell weighs 2,000 pounds it was around when the United States was still a baby. It rang out to signal holidays, meetings, and fire alarms. It cracked the first time it was used then it was fixed. Many years later, it cracked again this time it could not be fixed. The old bell is on display near Independence Hall in Philadelphia it no longer rings, but it's still the bell of freedom.”

…You can implement the following strategies:

Separate the independent clauses into sentences.

Have you ever seen the Liberty Bell? It's big, it's old, and it's cracked. The Liberty Bell weighs 2,000 pounds. It was around when the United States was still a baby. It rang out to signal holidays, meetings, and fire alarms. It cracked the first time it was used. Then, it was fixed. Many years later, it cracked again. This time it could not be fixed. The old bell is on display near Independence Hall in Philadelphia. It no longer rings, but it's still the bell of freedom.

Restructure the sentence by subordinating one of the clauses.

Have you ever seen the 2000-pound Liberty Bell? It's big, it's old, and it's cracked. It was around when the United States was still a baby. It rang out to signal holidays, meetings, and fire alarms. It cracked the first time it was used, but it was fixed. Many years later, it cracked again, but this time it could not be fixed. The old bell no longer rings, but is on display near Independence Hall in Philadelphia. It's still the bell of freedom.
