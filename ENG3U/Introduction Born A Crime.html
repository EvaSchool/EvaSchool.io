Speaking Your Truth Born A Crime Lawful Discrimination

The most remarkable stories of liberation result from applying one of Clint’s core principles: speaking’s one truth.

A prime example is how Nelson Mandela became the first democratically elected president in South Africa, breaking decades of racial oppression during apartheid. 

Born A Crime details what it is like to grow up in South Africa before and after the apartheid.

To help you imagine the author’s accounts of this period, we will take a few moments to understand apartheid, how it affected the people of South Africa, and its central figure, Nelson Mandela.

  • Apartheid is an Afrikaans word that translates to “separateness.”
  • Apartheid is a system of racial segregation that was enforced by the government of South Africa for nearly 50 years.

Under apartheid laws, South Africans had to register as members of one of four racial groups:

  • White
  • Black
  • Indians
  • Colored (people of “mixed” race) 

The treatment the individuals received, depended on the color of their skin.

                       

  • There were a total of 148 apartheid policies.

 

Can you spot which of these was true?

  • Black people had to carry identification at all times and obey strict curfews.
  • Interracial marriages were banned and punishable by law.
  • Your race dictated where individuals could live, work and go to school.
  • Black laborers were underpaid and prohibited from striking.
  • Non-whites did not have the right to vote. 

THESE POLICIES WERE ALL TRUE.   

                

Dismantling Apartheid

Resistance to these unjust apartheid laws came in the form of mostly non-violent political protests.

A key figure of this resistance was Nelson Mandela.

A New Hope

Mandela, along with other freedom fighters, called for the abolition of apartheid.

The government met this with increased sanctions for people caught breaking the law, which then led to Mandela’s arrest.

The path to democracy was undoubtedly long and hard-fought, beginning from direct opposition to armed exiled struggle.

A tide of global pressure, coupled with continuous civil unrest, propelled negotiations to dismantle apartheid.

In 1990, after 27 years of imprisonment, Nelson Mandela was released.

Four years later, he became president of South Africa.

“I Am Prepared to Die”

Listen to a snippet of his famous

“I Am Prepared to Die” speech.

 

“I have fought against white domination, and I have fought against black domination. I have cherished the ideal of a democratic and free society in which all persons live together in harmony and with equal opportunities. It is an ideal which I hope to live for and to achieve. But if needs be, it is an ideal for which I am prepared to die.”
