Types of Sentences

What Makes a Sentence?

  • A group of words that express a complete thought is called a sentence.

  

Types of Sentences by Function

There are four types of sentences under this category:

Declarative Exclamatory

Declarative

Declarative sentences are statements.

  • The function of this sentence is to state or describe something.
  • It ends with a period ( . )

Examples:

  • My phone is missing.
  • She is about to turn 18 years old.

Exclamatory

These are sentences that show strong emotion.

  • They usually end with an exclamation point ( ! )

Examples:

  • This book is very interesting!
  • The view was so breathtaking!

Interrogative

Interrogative sentences are essentially questions.

  • They end in a question mark (?)

Examples:

  • “Are you referring to me?”
  • “When will you call me?”

Imperative

When someone commands or requests you to do something, the statement they give is called an imperative sentence.

  • Imperative sentences end with either an exclamation point or period (depending on the intended meaning).

Examples:

  • Bring me a glass of cold water.
  • Don’t stay out at night.
  • Never cheat on tests!

   

Categorizing Sentences by Structure

Now that you know the types of sentences by function, let’s now explore the types of sentences by structure.

  • First, we need to understand the concept of clauses.

Clauses

  • Clauses are combinations of words and phrases that give either a complete or incomplete thought.
  • Usually, you know it is a clause when a subject and a verb appear together.

   

Simple Sentence Compound Sentence Complex Sentence Compound-Complex Sentence

This type contains 1 independent clause.

  • It expresses a complete thought.

Examples:

  • You know how to drive.
  • The group will need a great leader.

A compound sentence is a sentence that has at least two independent clauses joined by either a semicolon, comma, or a conjunction.

Examples:

  • We don’t need any more props, and we don’t have to show you any list of it.
  • Today I consider myself the luckiest woman on the face of the earth; I just got into my dream school!

If the sentence contains one independent clause and at least one dependent clause, this sentence is a complex sentence.

Examples:

  • Because I left my phone in class, I will have to go back to the school.
  • If you cook it, she will come.

Basically, this is a combination of a compound and complex sentence.
