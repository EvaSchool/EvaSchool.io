Parallel Structure

Parallel structure means using the same pattern of words to show that two or more ideas have the same level of importance.

This can happen at any level: a single word, phrase, or entire clause.

The usual way to join parallel structures is with the use of coordinating conjunctions such as “and” or “or.”

Callout

PARALLELISM IN WORDS AND PHRASES

Words and phrases can be joined with either the –ing form (called a gerund) or the infinitive form.

  • Mary likes hiking, to swim, and biking.  NOT PARALLEL 
  • Mary likes hiking, swimming, and biking. PARALLEL
  • Mary likes to hike, swim, and bike.   PARALLEL

The students love to study, playing, and chatting. NOT PARALLEL

Andrew is skilled in singing, dancing, and acting. PARALLEL

I would spend my free time either daydreaming or sleeping. PARALLEL

To see is believing. NOT PARALLEL

PARALLELISM IN WORDS AND PHRASES

A parallel structure that begins with a clause must keep using clauses. Changing to another pattern or changing the form of the verb will break the parallelism.

  • The coach told the players that they should get a lot of sleep, that they should not eat too much, and to do some warm-up exercises before the game. NOT PARALLEL 
  • The coach told the players that they should get a lot of sleep, that they should not eat too much, and that they should do some warm-up exercises before the game. PARALLEL
  • The coach told the players that they should get a lot of sleep, not eat too much, and do some warm-up exercises before the game. PARALLEL

PARALLELISM AFTER COLONS OR BULLETED LIST

Be sure to keep all the elements in a list in the same form.

  • The dictionary can be used for these purposes: to find word meanings, pronunciations, correct spellings, and looking up irregular verbs. NOT PARALLEL 
  • The dictionary can be used for these purposes: to find word meanings, pronunciations, correct spellings, and irregular verbs. PARALLEL
