Conjunctions and Clauses

Conjunctions

Conjunctions are a part of speech. They link words together with other words, phrases, or clauses.

  • Conjunctions allow you to create complex sentences by connecting trains of thought.

There are three types of conjunctions:

  • Coordinating conjunctions
  • Correlative conjunctions
  • Subordinating conjunctions

Coordinating Conjunctions

  • There are seven coordinating conjunctions.
  • These are used between phrases, clauses, or words.

Remember the acronym:

F.A.N.B.O.Y.S.

(For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So)

Callout

F.A.N.B.O.Y.S

“For” is used when explaining the reason for something.

  • The wet floor sign is for warning people to avoid serious injury from walking on a wet floor.

“And” is used when adding a phrase, clause, or word to another phrase, clause, or word.

  • I decided to take her on a date and then, pick up our kids.

“Nor” is used when adding a phrase or clause with a negative idea to another phrase or clause with a negative idea.

  • The kid didn’t like the ice cream, nor the fries that go with it.&nbsp;

“But” is used to show differences between two things.

  • Jessie has a special talent in singing but has problems with her academics.&nbsp;

“Or” is used to show an alternative.

  • You can go to a cafe, or tour the city.

“Yet” is used to provide a contrast to the first statement.

  • Luna keeps saying that she wants to stay in the house, yet she is fully dressed up to go out.

“So” is used to show a result of an event. It is also used to show the consequence of something.

Correlative Conjunctions

  • These are pairs of conjunctions that are used together to connect two phrases or clauses.
  • Common correlative conjunctions are:
    • Either/or - this is used in a sentence to show that two things are of the same level and are interchangeable.
    • Neither/nor - this is used in a sentence to show that two or more negative alternatives. It is used in a negative sense.
    • Not only/but also - this is used to connect two phrases, clauses, or words in the same level.

Correlative Conjunctions

Either/or We can either stay home or go to the amusement park.
Nether/nor Neither my sister nor my brother wanted to go to the museum with me.
Not only/but also He is not only good at swimming but also gets high marks in Math.

 

More Correlative Conjunctions

  • These are other examples of conjunctions that come in pairs. They link parts of sentences together using as/as, or both/and
  • as/as is used to compare two things.
    • “She was as tall as her brother.”
    • As + adjective + as
  • both/and is used to show that two things are equal.
    • “Tonya was both a sister and a mother to her friends.”

SUBORDINATE CONJUNCTIONS

  • These conjunctions begin subordinate clauses. A subordinate clause has both a subject and a verb, yet it cannot stand alone as a sentence.
  • Subordinate conjunctions include (but are not limited to) these words: 
    • although
    • since
    • if
    • because
    • until
    • when
    • whenever
    • before (sometimes)
    • after (sometimes)

Examples of SUBORDINATE CONJUNCTIONS

  • Although I am small, I am strong. (the subordinate clause begins with although).
  • Because I have no money, I cannot go to the movies. (the subordinate clause begins with because).
  • I cannot get my license until I turn sixteen. (the subordinate clause begins with until).

Phrases are group of words.

Clauses 

Clauses are combinations of words and phrases that give either complete or incomplete thoughts.

Usually, you know it is a clause when a subject and a verb appear together. A complete sentence requires both a subject and a verb.

There are Two main types of Clauses: Independent clauses & Dependent clauses

Independent Clause

This type of clause can stand alone because it presents a complete thought or idea.

Examples:

  • One of the best movies of all time is “Titanic.”
  • Leonardo Dicaprio is undeniably a good actor. 
  • She spoke. (short independent clause)
  • She spoke loudly to the class, trying to be heard over the noise. (long independent clause)

Dependent Clause

The type of clause shows an incomplete thought or idea and needs an independent clause for it to make sense.

These are also known as subordinate clauses

Examples: the highlighted portions are dependent clauses

  • If you ask me, popcorn is always necessary when watching movies.
  • Actors need to choose their movies carefully, to get nominated for Oscars.

Noun Clauses

  • This dependent clause functions as a noun. 
  • This means that it is as a person, place or idea as part of any sentence (as a subject, object or complement).

Examples:

  • The pancake she made is her favorite. (subject)
  • The daughter asked her mother how she could cook her father’s favorite dish. (direct object)

Adjective Clauses

  • This dependent clause functions as an adjective. This means that it describes a noun or pronoun.
  • It usually starts with a relative pronoun (who, which, whose, when, where, that)

Examples:

  • Jenny, who is a member of Black Pink, was the best performer I’ve seen live.
  • Black Pink, which is a famous KPOP group, is going to have a concert this January.

Adverb Clauses

  • This dependent clause functions as an adverb.
  •  This means that it describes the main verb or adjectives in the sentence.
