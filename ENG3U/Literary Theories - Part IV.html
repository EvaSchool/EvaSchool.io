Reader Response  Theory

This theory focuses on how the reader perceives the text instead of what the author intends. 

The text itself has no meaning until a reader reads it.

Applying the Reader Response Theory

  • With this theory, the reader takes an active role in interpreting the text.
  • As a critic, you have to examine the scope and variety of reader reactions, then analyze how different readers, sometimes called “interpretive communities,” make meaning out of the text. 
  • Purely personal reactions and inherited or culturally conditioned ways of reading, may affect the interpretation of the text.

Applying the Theory to “Cinderella”

  • When the work was released for Cinderella, readers included women of this period who mostly stayed at home and had maids - similar to Cinderella before she went to the ball. 
  • These readers related to the text because of the unbelievable thought of being free for some time, and having a fairy godmother to make them beautiful for a night.
  • These women likely wanted an escape from their everyday lives, and therefore relate to the story this way.

Reader Response  Theory

This is the easiest out of all the theories. 

There is no right or wrong answer because what matters is your understanding of the text. 

Your purpose is to examine, explain and defend your reaction to the text.

DO

  • Do write as an educated adult addressing other adults. Use examples when criticizing text.
  • Do criticize the text either from principle (e.g. Does the text unreasonably put down hot topics such as religion, women, politics, etc.?); or form (e.g. Is the text poorly written? Are there typos? Are the ideas disorganized?)

DO NOT

  • Do not settle for using: “I liked this text because it is amazing and it made me feel good.” or “I hated it because it is so boring and I can’t relate to it.”
  • Instead, take an analytical approach to the reading.
  • Do not summarize the contents of the text at length. You can assume that the reader has already read the text in question.

Choose the Text to Study

Be sure to read it closely so you can truly connect with it.

Write the Introduction

Make sure that you mention the title of the work, the author, and your central thesis.

Answer These Questions for the Body

  • What does the text have to do with you, personally, and with your past or present experiences?
  • How much does the text agree or clash with your worldview?
  • What did you learn, and how much were your views and opinions challenged or changed by this text, if at all?

Wrap it All Up in the Conclusion

  • How well does the text address things that you care about?
  • What can you praise about the text? What problems do you have with it? 
  • How well did you enjoy the text (or not)?
