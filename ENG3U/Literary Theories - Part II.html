Biographical and Psychological

In this lesson, we will focus on two other different literary theories: 

The Biographical Theory Lens

  • This literary theory focuses on the author’s life and experiences.
  • To apply this theory, we will rely on a close examination of character and motivations and their symbolic meanings (impulses, desires, feelings) that might be linked with the author.

Questions to Ask With a Biographical Approach

  • To what extent are the events described in work a direct transfer of what happened in the writer’s actual life?
  • What modifications of the actual events has the writer made in the literary work? For what possible purposes? 
  • What are the effects of the differences between actual events and their literary transformation in the poem, story, play, or essay?
  • What has the author revealed in work about his/her characteristic modes of thought, perception, or emotion? What place does this work have in the artist’s literary development and career?

Reminder for Biographical Approach

  • One must also be careful not to take the biographical facts of a writer’s life too far to criticize that writer’s works.
  • Only use the insight provided by knowledge of the author’s life.
  • Remember, the biographical data should amplify the text’s meaning, not drown it out with irrelevant material.

Biographical Theory on “The Black Cat” by Edgar Allan Poe

Read:https://poestories.com/read/blackcat 

In the story, “The Man” (the narrator), has the same character as the writer. Poe also married at a young age. He married his wife, who was fourteen years old at that time. The marriage proved a happy one, and the family was said to have enjoyed singing together at night. Virginia (Poe’s wife) expressed her devotion to her husband in a Valentine poem now in the Enoch Pratt Free Library collection, and Poe celebrated the joys of married life in his poem “Eulalie.” Both Poe and “The Man” married early and had a happy family life. Both of them married, first had pets, and are alcoholics. Poe reflects some part of his life in “The Black Cat.”

The Psychological Theory Lens

  • This theory examines how the author’s emotional upbringing or psychological history impacts a text.
  • The psychological theory in literature frequently applies Freudian psychology in determining the author's influence on the work.

Freudian Application to Psychological Theory

According to Freudian psychology, there are three parts to an individual's psyche that subconsciously affect one's personality: Id, Ego and Superego.

Id

"I want to do it now!"

Id sought immediate gratification and expressed impulses, desires and instincts.

Part #2 of an Individual's Psyche (Freud)

Superego

On the other hand, Superego uses ideals and morals to present one's ideal version of self to the world.

Ego

"Maybe we can compromise."

The ego goes as a mediator between Id and Superego.

Psychological Theory on “The Black Cat” by Edgar Allan Poe

Poe’s alcoholism reflected on the narrator for the story. Edgar Allan Poe presents the protagonist, who is the victim of complex mental issues. Poe portrays three main psychological aspects of the human mind that include irrationality, perversity and guilt. The narrator gradually changes from someone gentle to a murderer in the story. Although Poe does not provide a concrete explanation for the narrator’s encroaching loss of sanity, perhaps suggesting that madness might happen at any time to any person, the narrator admits the role of alcohol in his behaviour.
