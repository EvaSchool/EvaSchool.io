  • Social belonging, conformity to structures, and acceptable forms of behaviours are observed in primary groups. But what happens when a young person conforms to a group that is not sanctioned by society and adheres to behaviour that is contrary to public modes of interaction?
  • Social psychologists are equally interested in this type of behaviour which they regard as deviant.  
  • Deviance is a type of behaviour that violates the standard of conduct or expectations of a group or society. It usually elicits a negative response from the mainstream group. 

Deviants and Robert Merton’s Social Strain Typology

  • American sociologist Robert Merton (1910-2003) provided one of the most known theories of deviance. 
  • He outlined several typologies of deviant behaviour and his strain theory became the standard in sociological research about deviant behavior.
  • For Merton, society itself causes deviance. Individuals are pressured to conform within the standards of society, but some may find other ways of doing it. 
  • Individuals may have a break from the mainstream culture and seek alternative means of achieving a goal or expressing individuality.
  • Deviance is viewed as a way of envisioning the same outcome but through divergent means. This may lead the individual to participate in subcultures.
  • A subculture is an alternative system of values and beliefs that does not conform to the mainstream cultural and social norms. 
  • Since adolescence is a natural time of self-exploration, some are drawn to engage in subculture to experience social belonging in a group of like-minded individuals who share a particular taste in music, arts, fashion. Some examples are cybergoth or hip hop subcultures. 
  • As long as deviant subcultures do not represent a criminal element, they can co-exist with the mainstream society with very little conflict.  
  • In fact they may even provide a major impetus for positive social change, examples are civil rights activism, feminism, gay rights activism.  It only says that not all divergent thinking is negative and aims at toppling the mainstream culture.

Conformity

This type of deviance involves individuals adhering to both the cultural goals and the approved means of achieving them. In other words, they accept the societal norms and strive to achieve success through legitimate channels.

Ritualism

In contrast to conformity, ritualism occurs when individuals abandon the pursuit of societal goals while continuing to follow the established means. They may go through the motions of adhering to social norms but lack motivation or ambition to achieve the desired goals.

Innovation

Innovation involves accepting societal goals but pursuing them through unconventional or illegal means. Individuals in this category may seek success and social status but resort to activities considered deviant or criminal to attain their objectives.

Retreatism

Retreatism occurs when individuals reject both societal goals and the means to achieve them. They withdraw from mainstream society and may adopt alternative lifestyles or engage in substance abuse as a way of coping with their disconnection from societal norms.

Rebellion

This type of deviance is characterized by rejecting societal goals and means while actively seeking to replace them with alternative goals and means. Individuals in this category challenge existing social structures and norms, advocating for radical changes to the status quo.

 

These five types of deviance offer insight into the various ways individuals respond to societal expectations and pressures, highlighting the complexity of human behavior within social systems.

Deviants and Robert Merton’s Social Strain Typology

  • Conformists are people who accept the cultural goals of society as well as the means to achieve those goals. 
  • They will always abide by the rules set out for them by society (e.g., undergoing through the institutionalized career paths to become a doctor, lawyer, teacher).
  • Ritualists accept the means or standards to achieve the end goal but may not necessarily accept the cultural goals of society, they may even reject them (e.g., a restaurateur might open a successful restaurant but may have no desire to establish a restaurant chain).

  • Innovators are unique in their own way by thinking outside the box and challenging the way things are done. While their goals are in line with society’s, they strive to achieve them through alternative means.

  • They establish new modes of behaviour and standards within their field (e.g., some of the greatest innovators in the field of technology are Bill Gates, Steve Jobs, and Mark Zuckerberg).
  • Retreatists are individuals who choose to disengage from the mainstream culture altogether. They do not agree with the common cultural goals and as a result they prefer to live among like-minded people.
  • They believe that the standard rules and norms of society do not speak to them (e.g., the hippie culture in North America that emerged in the 60s and 70s which rejected the mainstream culture of the day).
  • Like the retreatists, rebels also openly reject societal cultural goals as well as the means to attain those goals but they do not isolate themselves. 
  • They attempt to transform society in which they live by challenging and recreating the cultural norms and goals that govern their respective societies particularly in the social, political, economic, religious realms.
  • In the past, rebels were synonymous with paramilitary and extremely violent groups within society who engaged in warfare against despotic leaders.
