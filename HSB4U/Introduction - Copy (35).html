Crime

         

Lesson Description

In this lesson on crime, we'll explore social science theories like functionalism, strain theory, control theory, symbolic interactionism, and conflict theory to understand why individuals engage in criminal behavior. We'll analyze statistical data on social deviance, discrimination, and hate crimes, discussing methods of deterrence used by institutions and societies and examining the relationship between social panic about crime and deviance and media and political attention. Lastly, we'll explore conflicts between deterring social deviance and maintaining civil liberties, assessing the challenges of balancing crime control measures with individual rights in a democratic society.

Overall Expectations

C3 Social Deviance: demonstrate an understanding of social science theories about social deviance, and of how various responses to deviance affect individuals and society.

Specific Expectations

  • C3.1 Describe the key ideas of major social sci­ence theories related to deviance.
  • C3.2 Summarize and interpret statistics related to social deviance, discrimination, and hate crimes. 
  • C3.3 Describe various methods of deterrence used within local, national, and global institutions and societies.
  • C3.4 Explain the relationship between social panic about crime and deviance and the attention given to these issues by media, politicians, and other social groups.
  • C3.5 Explain ways in which the deterrence of social deviance and the maintenance of civil liberties can come into conflict with one another.

Learning Goals

  • Understand the systems of social stratification and the social science theories on social inequality in Canada.
  • Appreciate and describe the Canadian criminal justice system and social justice movements throughout the world.
  • Acquire the research and inquiry skills of evaluating and citing sources of information in any social science study.
  • Learn about volunteer opportunities and ways to work toward social justice.
