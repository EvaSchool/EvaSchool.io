The Class System in Canada: Is There Such a Thing?

Every society has its own groups of people who share the same values, norms, lifestyles, education and types of work attitudes.

These groups are often divided into different social classes and they possess varying degrees of influence and power, some have more than the others. A person is outright given an ascribed status by virtue of race, gender, or age.

Many believe that today’s society has since allowed its people to have an acquired status through meritocracy where they earn rewards through personal merit. The basic assumption is that everyone begins with an equal opportunity, so everyone based on merit can achieve a higher status. 

In reality however, there are barriers to achieving a higher status:

1. A student from a poor family who could not afford to pursue higher education because of lack of financial means.

2. The difficulties being faced by those who were assigned an ascribed status that prevents them from getting the best jobs - women, aboriginal people, those with disabilities, those belonging to ethnic groups, older or younger individuals - they are often at a disadvantage simply because of who they are.

There is a comforting widespread notion that as a free country, Canada is a “classless” society. That everyone has an equal opportunity regardless of gender, race, ethnicity, or financial background.

A closer look however, would reveal otherwise. A class structure actually exists in Canada as society becomes more complex. Canada has four major social groups existing: upper class, middle class, working class and lower class.

Upper Class

The upper class in Canada comprises around 3-5% of the population. They own substantial amounts of wealth which is largely derived from inheritance although some members of the upper class have derived their own fortunes.

The expensive schools they go to do not only give them the best education but the advantage of a network of influential connections. The positions they occupy provide them a great deal of influence on and control over the economy and politics.

Middle Class

The middle class is the largest social class in Canada. They comprise 40 - 50% of the Canadian population. Their sheer size wields a great deal of influence on trends and patterns in Canadian culture.

Television and other forms of mass media often portray middle class families and most commercial advertising is directed at them because they represent a widespread and powerful group of viewers and consumers. 

They are traditionally involved in professions such as medicine and law but have grown to include office managers and employees, factory owners, and other professions that could afford a better lifestyle. They are referred to as the “white collar” workers receiving higher salaries with generous benefits and most are within the public sector.&nbsp;

Working Class

For Marx, the working class are those who sell their skills for money. Nowadays, his definition has become too narrow since almost everyone works for someone else.&nbsp;

The working class comprise about 30% of Canadian population. They are found in factories or occupations like technicians, mechanics, tradespeople. Because of the manual labor they do, they are known as “blue collar” workers. 

Some notable changes that have occurred in recent years are worth noting which have made lines between the middle and working classes unclear:

  • Some working class jobs require little formal training but other jobs like electricians, plumbers, demand a high degree of technical certification.
  • Traditionally, working-class jobs paid less than middle-class ones, but it is no longer always the case today where a skilled worker may earn more than an office worker.
  • They enjoy many consumer goods and services available to the middle class.
    Their children go to the university or trade schools as do the children of the middle-class families.

Lower Class

Occupying the lowest strata of society are the members of the lower class. They make up 15 to 20% of Canadian population.  Their dire financial situation makes life unstable and insecure for them. They may work in part-time jobs but the income they generate is enough to cover necessities.

Some may be supported by the state through welfare payments.

Some may have barriers in accessing better work because of disability - physical or mental, lack of training, age barriers or family responsibilities.
