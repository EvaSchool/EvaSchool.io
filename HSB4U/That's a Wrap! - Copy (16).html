What have I learned?

 

In conclusion, the study of internal drives, exemplified by Émile Durkheim's anomie and Sigmund Freud's psychoanalytic theory, provides valuable insights into societal dynamics and human behavior within the broader context of theories explaining social change.

Durkheim's concept highlights the consequences of unclear social norms on societal cohesion, reflecting various psychological, social, material, and cultural conditions driving social change. Similarly, Freud's exploration of instincts underscores the interplay between unconscious drives and conscious decision-making, emphasizing the psychological factors shaping societal shifts. 

Understanding social change necessitates analyzing economic, environmental, political, and sociocultural factors, as well as exploring various means of creating social change and their impacts on individuals and Canadian society, including relationships between conformity, alienation, and societal transformation, along with the implications of poverty and affluence. Thus, integrating these perspectives enhances our understanding of the complex nature of social change and its effects on Canadian society.
