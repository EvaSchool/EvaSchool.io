Generational Theories

Generational Theories

  • Generational theories provide an explanation of how society changes over time by looking at the relationships between generations.
  • It reveals the dominant values and attitudes held by each group and how these brought positive or negative influences from one generation to the next.
  • The time a person is born provides a huge influence on his/her world view.  
  • It also says that people of the same cohort or statistical group (e.g., age, birth) generally share the same value systems which form the basis for their personal and social behaviour.

The more notable generational theories are offered by the following sociologists and historians:

  • Fresh Contacts (Karl Mannheim)
  • Strauss-Howe Generational Theory (William Strauss & Neil Howe)
  • Generational Replacement

Karl Mannheim and Fresh Contacts

  • German sociologist Karl Mannheim (1893-1947) coined the term “fresh contact” which is a young person’s personal interpretation of the world as they experience it which may not necessarily align with the views of the older generations.
  • Young people learn important values from their parents and local communities.
  • The entire group comes to share similar core ideals and will continue to hold these values throughout their collective lifetime.
  • Developed the idea of “social location” or the influence of birth year on a person’s attitudes, values, and views.
  • Believed that sharing a common space in history, the social action and social views throughout a person’s lifetime will be inextricably linked to those of his or her generation.

Strauss-Howe Generational Theory



William Strauss (1947-2007)

  • American historians William Strauss and Neil Howe identified a repeating cycle in generational values that alternate between four distinct archetypes (symbol or pattern) which they called “turnings” - the Prophet, Nomad, Hero, and Artist generations.

Neil Howe (1951-)

  • According to their theory, during each 80-year period, each of four turnings emerges, becomes popular, and gives way to the next.

William Strauss, an American historian born in 1947, alongside his collaborator Neil Howe, contributed significantly to the development of the Strauss-Howe Generational Theory. This theory highlights a cyclical pattern in generational values, which are classified into four distinct archetypes known as "turnings": Prophet, Nomad, Hero, and Artist generations. Through their research, Strauss and Howe observed that these turnings alternate over an 80-year period, with each one emerging, gaining prominence, and eventually transitioning to the next. This cyclical nature of generational values reflects the dynamic shifts in societal norms and cultural trends. Neil Howe, born in 1951, worked closely with William Strauss to identify and define these generational patterns, providing valuable insights into the recurring cycles of societal change and cultural evolution.

Strauss-Howe Generational Theory

Look at this scenario:

  • The Silent Generation, grew up during the Great Depression and World War II, they are said to be inner-directed.
  • Baby Boom Generation, started with idealism but then gave way to economic hardships and disillusionment with social structures and institutions.
  • Generation X, grew up in the shadow of the boomers.
  • Millennial Generation, witnessed the on-going war on terrorism and the era of globalization.
  • If their theory is correct, this current generation, the Generation Z stands to witness an event that will cause social upheaval and then respond for renewal to occur.

Generational Replacement

  • This theory claims that changes in beliefs and attitudes of young people are important markers of long-term social change.
  • The ideas and values they acquire during adolescence will continue to shape their worldview well into adulthood and potentially for life.
  • A good example is that young people’s views on environmental issues can predict future social trends on such issues by potentially influencing their future generation.
