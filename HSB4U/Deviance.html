Deviance

 

A type of behavior that violates the standard of conduct or expectations of a group or society. This type of behavior is seen as a significant factor that drives society to change.

Deviance, however, varies from culture to culture.  An act of deviance in one culture may be considered normal in another.  

 

Some examples of variances may be based on a number of factors:

Location

Location - speaking loudly at a party may be considered normal but not when attending church service or watching a movie.

Age 

Age - a two-year old crying at a supermarket may be considered normal but not for an adult.

Social status

Social status - famous individuals (politicians, actors) may get special favors for a particular service, but ordinary persons have to wait for their turn.

Individual societies

Individual societies - in some societies, being on time is strictly observed, but in some, people are used to waiting and giving some time allowance to others. A more controversial example is the acceptance of abortion in some cultures while it is unacceptable to some.

  

Sometimes deviance does not have to be shown in manners or behaviors but in the appearance of individuals. They may have some physical traits or abilities that are not considered normal and distinguish them from other members of society.

Sociologist Erving Goffman (1922-1982) used the term stigma to describe the labels society uses to devalue members of certain social groups. 

When persons are stigmatized, it means that society has given them a mark that is often laden with disgrace. Stigmas against social groups however may change over time. What was once stigmatized as deviant may become accepted and/or mainstreamed through time.

Some examples are piercings, tattoos, and people with mental disorders. While society’s treatment of those with mental disorders has improved over time, some stigma attached to them remains no matter how subtle they may be.

 

Social Control

Violations of social norms are dealt with through various forms of social control. It consists of ways to promote conformity to societal norms and they are important to make social life more predictable.

Social control has two broad types: internal and external

  

Internal social control External social control
  • Developed during the socialization process and lies with the individual.
  • Doing or not doing something according to what you believe is right or wrong.
  • Happens when socialization that lies within the individual fails to ensure conformity to social norms.
  • The society’s efforts to bring those who “step outside the line” back into line.
  • Made up of a system of rewards to encourage conformity and punishments to stop socially acceptable behavior.
  • Rewards may be considered positive sanctions such as smiles, words of approval, or awards.
  • Punishments or negative sanctions may come in the form of criticisms, fines, or even imprisonment.&nbsp;

  

Informal sanctions are rewards or punishments that any member of the group may apply like thanking someone for their help or ridiculing for their behavior, gossiping, or making facial expressions.

Formal sanctions are rewards or punishments that are only imposed by people who are given special authority to do so (e.g., when a judge hands down a life sentence in prison to a convicted prisoner or when a teacher rewards a student for earning outstanding grades).
