Crime

Crime is a deviation from social norms and an act or omission that constitutes an offense may be subject to formal penalties. Just like deviance, what is considered a crime varies by time and place. For example in Canada, the legal smoking age and age to buy alcohol is 18 in some provinces while 19 in others.

Crime also tends to affect some groups more than others. It can also be gender-specific such as in Canada, women and girls represent the majority of sexual assault victims, forcible confinement, kidnapping, and harrasment.

Likely offenders

Sociologists classify likely crime offenders according to age, gender, social class, race, and ethnicity, or the very same factors where inequality exists.

The age of a person committing a criminal offense is one of the most significant factors associated with crime. Generally, arrests start among early adolescence, peak in young adulthood, and steadily decline with age.

In Canada, there are two separate justice systems depending on age, the one for young offenders (12 to 17 years) and adults (18 and above). The rationale given by the Department of Justice for having these two systems is due to the premise that while the youth should be held accountable for their crimes, they still lack the maturity of adults to fully understand the nature of their actions.

Another correlation consistently given to crimes is gender where most are committed by males. Some information reported by Statistics Canada for 2017 include the following:

  • Females account for 25% of all individuals accused in police-reported criminal incidents.
  • The number of female offenders is highest among the 18 to 24 age group and tends to decline with age.
  • Property crime accounted for the largest proportion (35%) of crime for which females were accused, while drug violations (7%) and other federal statutes (4%) accounted for the smallest.
  • Assault made up the vast majority (70%) of violent crimes committed by females.

Sociologists have opposing perspectives on whether or not social class is relevant to crime. Many of them assume that crime is economically motivated and that poverty will lead to criminal behaviour. This assumption is strengthened by research finding that people from lower economic backgrounds are more likely to commit violent and property crimes. White-collar and corporate crimes on the other hand are more likely to be committed by the upper class. They are, however, not reported as much until recently when they have been attracting more attention than in the past. White-collar crimes involve embezzlement, price fixing, stock market manipulation by affluent people or by professionals/business people.

In a heterogeneous society, some ethnic and racial groups will have higher crime rates than others. This is evident in the United States where African-Americans and Hispanics dominate the arrest data.  Correlating crime with race and ethnicity is not routinely done by Statistics Canada, therefore there is very little known about a comparative situation in Canada.  The textbook presents some information from Correctional Service Canada though these were from 2001 and 2007. The information says that black and First Nations people are arrested in disproportionate numbers. This is likely due to discrimination by the justice system against minority groups. While this type of discrimination may be unintentional, it is nonetheless apparent.
