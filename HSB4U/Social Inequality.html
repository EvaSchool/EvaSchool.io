Social Inequality

 

Social inequality exists when people from different social classes have varying degrees of access to money, wealth, status and power.

A person’s ability to access such factors are affected by a combination of different elements related to ethnicity and race, social background, and gender.

  

Economic inequality

Defined as the unequal distribution of wealth and income in a society.

Although income and wealth are used interchangeably, economists make an important distinction between the two.


Income - the amount of money one receives as wages or salaries during a given period

Wealth - refers to the abundance of valuable possessions or economic resources (land, property, cars, jewelry) owned by an individual or group. “Your income is your paycheck, your wealth is what you own.”

Even if the two concepts are different, they have a close relationship. Those with substantial income are the ones who are likely to accumulate wealth because of their disposable income. This is income that is above what is required for necessities such as food, clothing, and shelter. Inheritance (from parents or relatives) is another way for people to accumulate wealth.

For those who live in poverty, it is often impossible to accumulate wealth because there is none or very limited disposable income remaining after paying for necessities.

Canada is considered one of the wealthiest nations in the world, yet the country’s population is marked by inequalities in income and wealth.

The median after-tax income of Canadian families and unattached individuals was $61,400 in 2018 which was almost the same in 2017.

   

Poverty Absolute poverty Relative poverty

Determining wealth and income is fairly straightforward, but determining poverty is more complicated.

A way to define poverty is to ask what benefits people consider necessities in a society. If they do not possess these benefits, then they are considered poor. Poverty is classified into two categories, absolute and relative poverty.

Absolute poverty is the deprivation of resources that are considered essential - food, potable water, and housing.

People however may possess one (house) but still have not enough of the other (money for food or medical treatment).

Relative poverty measures the deprivation of some people against those who have more. This type of poverty considers how wealth and income are distributed.

A poverty line is set where those who live below it might be considered impoverished while those who live above the line are not.

Canada does not have an official poverty line. What it has as a means of identifying economic inequality is the low-income cut-off (LICO).

Statistics Canada uses the LICO as a way of determining how households spend on necessities - food, clothing, and shelter - to define who is poor. It changes every year due to inflation.  The LICO is also used as an immigration guideline to assess one’s financial capability to support a family member to come to Canada.

LICO is a statistical measure by Statistics Canada that identifies people who are substantially worse off than an average person - typically referred to as the “haves” and “have-nots”.
