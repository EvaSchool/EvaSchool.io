Socialization

Socialization

  • Socialization is the process by which individuals learn how to successfully function and participate in society. Children begin this process at home and continue to learn it in school.
  • Through social interaction, socialization allows individuals to learn to successfully operate in society by internalizing the values, norms and roles of that society. It allows individuals to fully function as human beings, able to fit into society.
  • This process is critical to both the individual and the society in which they live in. For a society to perpetuate itself, new members have to learn its culture, otherwise, it ceases to exist.
  • Culture is transmitted from one generation to the next. New generations have to learn the values and norms that are highly regarded by the group to which they have been born. They are expected to perform roles or behaviors assigned to them in their social positions.

The Lasting Influence of Family in Socialization

  • Socialization begins in the family.  As the child’s primary social group, the family plays an outstanding role in the socialization process.
  • It provides the child’s first system of values, beliefs and norms, which is usually a reflection of their own social status, religion, and race, among others.
  • Because of this, the family is regarded as the most important agent of socialization as it is the center of a child’s life and therefore provides the foundation of a child’s personality.
  • Positive or negative lessons taught by the family can have a lasting influence throughout an individual’s life.

The Growing Influence of Social Groups

  • As a person enters adolescence, one may find more social relationships that now extend beyond the family.
  • It is during this period when peers and social media contribute to young people’s understanding of the world and may cause changes to happen in their individual and social values, beliefs, and attitudes.
  • They become awakened to a new social reality by all unique forces surrounding them, both positive or negative. 
  • These forces provide many personal choices to teenagers, such as which classes to take; experimenting with illegal drugs, alcohol, smoking; becoming sexually active, which often make them seek input from their peers or social group. 
  • This is when they are conflicted between what they learned from their family and what their peers advise them to do, creating a psychological tug-of-war in the process.

Culture and Identity in Adolescence

  • Ethnic identity sets a person’s identification with or sense of belonging to an ethnic group.
  • Culture and ethnicity are important factors in how individuals see themselves during adolescence and onward.
  • The ease or difficulty with which adolescents belonging to an ethnic group find themselves achieving stable and healthy personalities depends on many factors, including confronting perceived or real discrimination or prejudice from the dominant culture.

Allison Davis and Socialized Anxiety

  • American social anthropologist and psychologist Allison Davis (1902-1983) pointed out that the socialization process includes a phenomenon known as “socialized anxiety”.
  • Socialized anxiety - the tension and discomfort felt by individuals that motivate and influence behaviour. 
  • A good example of this is the anxiety they feel about being accepted to college or university, which motivates them to earn good grades.
