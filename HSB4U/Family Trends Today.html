Family Trends Today

  • The traditional or nuclear family composed of husband, wife and children, no longer reflects the reality of many Canadian families. Modern family structures now consist of lone-parent, blended, same-sex and extended families.
  • This is an example of a social paradigm shift where the majority of the population pursues a specific social area other than the traditional way resulting in a change in widespread beliefs and behaviour.
  • In recent decades, the landscape of family dynamics has undergone significant transformation, deviating from the traditional nuclear model of husband, wife, and children. Contemporary Canadian families now exhibit diverse structures, including lone-parent households, blended families formed through remarriage, same-sex couples raising children, and extended family arrangements. This shift reflects a broader societal paradigm change, where the prevailing norms and behaviors within the population have evolved beyond the traditional framework, embracing a more varied and inclusive approach to family life. Such changes underscore the dynamic nature of social structures and the adaptability of familial relationships in response to shifting cultural, social, and legal landscapes.

Same-Sex Couples in Canada 2016

  • According to the 2016 Census, there were 72,280 same-sex couples in Canada in 2916m representing 0.9% of all couples.
  • From 2006 to 2016, the number of same-sex couples increased much more rapidly (+60.7%) than the number of opposite-sex couples (+9.6%).
  • One-third (33.4%) of all same-sex couples in Canada were married.
  • Although Ontario had the most same-sex couples (26,585), Quebec had the higher number relative to its population. Among provinces, Quebec also had the lowest percentage of married same-sex couples.
  • Half of all same-sex married couples in Canada were living in four of the country’s five largest metropolitan areas: Toronto, Montréal, Vancouver, and Ottawa-Gatineau.
  • About one in eight same-sex couples (12.0%) had children living with them in 2016, compared with about half of opposite-sex couples.
