External Factors Influencing Change

 

Changes in society and culture are influenced by a lot of external factors. The most pressing ones are:

  • Environment
  • Demography
  • Geography
  • Technology

Physical Environment

  • The physical environment is where society lives and thrives.
  • A small change in the physical environment has a ripple effect and may have a short or lasting impact on society.
  • Environmental issues are regarded as both man-made and natural. Man-made issues caused by humans include pollution and global warming.
  • Examples of natural disasters are flooding due to typhoons/hurricanes or tsunamis destroying agriculture, and communities and even the loss of human and animal life.

Population Changes

  • Birth and death used to be the main concern when it comes to changes in population.
  • In recent years, however, migration patterns (whether immigration or emigration) have been presenting significant implications for society and social change.
  • Through migration, communities are forced to meet the needs of their new members such as housing, education, jobs, and employment.
  • Aside from changes in the number per se of the population in a given area, society changes because of influences and interactions from diverse cultures resulting in new attitudes, behaviors, norms, and standards.
  • Communities that are losing their members through emigration have their challenges to battle, foremost of which is losing a good quality labor force or the so-called “brain drain.”

Proximity

  • Societies that are closely located to one another tend to experience more rapid and similar development than those that are remotely located. This is known as intercultural contact.
  • Historically, this happens during trade and war but nowadays, trade, globalization, and tourism - all made possible by modern technology, have made this phenomenon an everyday occurrence.

   

Social Environment, Culture, and Social Values

There are two dominant types of social structures that dictate changes in human culture:

The Individualist The Collectivist
  • Individualist societies on the other hand value autonomy, freedom, and personal rights. They can tolerate cultural diversity and are more receptive to change.
  • The collectivist cultures value conformity, uniformity, and cooperation. Personal needs, privileges, and freedoms are secondary only to group needs.

When it comes to social values, two opposing factors determine how society accepts change:

  1. Larger, post-industrialist societies are said to be more likely to accept innovations and embrace change.
    Smaller and more isolated societies for their part are more reluctant to accept change.
  2. Traditions, customs, and rituals still dominate their way of life and therefore they exhibit ethnocentrism, which is the belief that one’s own culture or ethnic group is superior to all others.

   

Technology

The influence of technology in societal changes has been so formidable in recent years, that is why it deserves a discussion all its own.

The pervasive influence of technology on societal changes has become increasingly apparent in recent years, warranting a dedicated discussion to explore its multifaceted impact.

Sociologist William Felding Ogburn (1886-1959) proposed a seminal idea that underscores the significance of technology as the primary mechanism driving progress in society. Ogburn's assertion serves as a foundational concept in understanding the transformative power of technology across various domains of human existence.

From communication and transportation to healthcare, education, and beyond, technology has revolutionized the way individuals interact, work, and live their lives. Its rapid evolution and adoption have reshaped social structures, cultural norms, and economic systems, leading to both unprecedented advancements and complex challenges.

By examining the intricate interplay between technology and societal dynamics, we can gain deeper insights into the profound implications of technological innovation on the fabric of modern civilization.
