Evaluating and citing sources

Credible and accurate information are the hallmarks of good social science research. Every source of information must be evaluated to determine its quality and relevance to the research topic.

Citing your sources of information in a correct manner lends credibility to your research. You are required to cite your sources every time you use direct quotations, paraphrase, summarize or refer to another person’s work.

Evaluating Sources

  • Evaluating the authority, accuracy, and reliability of the information from secondary sources is a crucial step in the research process.
  • How do you recognize a high quality source of information, whether in print or from the Internet?  You have to ask a number of questions about the sources of your research such as books, periodicals, multimedia resources, and websites.

Citing Sources

  • Proper citation of sources of information is a must in any research. Any information quoted, paraphrased, summarized in the paper must be acknowledged and documented with a citation to avoid plagiarism.
  • Among the different citation styles, the American Psychological Association (APA) style is commonly used in social science research.

This is a word-for-word information that is directly taken from a certain source. It has to be exactly referenced from where it is taken.

  1. Quotations of less than 40 words should be incorporated in the text and enclosed with double quotation marks. Provide the author, publication year, and a page number in parentheses after the quotation.
  2. Alternatively, if the author/s are cited within the same sentence, place the publication year after the author, in parentheses and place the page number after the quotation.
  3. When the quotation is more than 40 words, use a free-standing block on a new line, indented five spaces, and omit the quotation marks. 

When using your own words to refer indirectly to another author’s work, the original source must be identified.  A complete reference must appear at the end of the paper.

    1. In most cases, providing the author’s last name and the publication year are sufficient. Within a paragraph, you do not need to include the year in subsequent references.
    2. If there are two authors, include the last name of each and the publication year.
    3. If there are three to five authors, cite all authors the first time; in subsequent citations, include only the last name of the first author followed by “et al.” and the year.
    4. The names of groups that serve as authors (e.g., corporations, associations, government agencies) are usually spelled out each time they appear in a text citation. If it will not confuse the reader, the organization’s name may be abbreviated.
    5. For electronic sources that do not provide page numbers, use the paragraph number, if available, preceded by the ¶ symbol or abbreviation “para.” If neither is visible, cite the heading and the number of the paragraph following it to direct the reader to the quoted material.

Your reference should appear alphabetically at the end of your paper. This list should contain the complete information about the sources you used. It allows your readers to refer to them as needed.

  1. The list of references must be on a new page at the end of your text.
  2. The title “Reference” should be centered at the top of the page.
  3. Entries are arranged alphabetically by author’s last name or by title if there is no author.
  4. Titles of larger works (i.e., books, journals, encyclopedias) are italicized.
  5. Entries are double-spaced.
  6. For each entry, the first line is typeset flush with the left margin. Additional lines are indented as a group a few spaces to the right of the left margin (a hanging indent).

 

 Social Injustice and Inequality

  • Society is a large group of people with shared values, who are living and interacting together in an organized way. In an ideal society, members are supposed to be working together for the common good.
  • All societies, however, are characterized by social stratification where people are allocated a hierarchy based on wealth, status and power.
  • Canadian society, just like any other societies in the world, is an example of a stratified society.
  • In Canada, access to and control over wealth, status, and power are restricted by a person’s social background, gender, ethnicity, and religion. This situation divides Canadian society into distinct and unequal groups.  Let us examine the forces at work that lead to social stratification and inequality in society. 

Social Stratification

  • As societies become complex due to the creation of numerous structures, inequality becomes inevitable. Complex societies begin to elevate groups of people above others.
  • Social scientists are interested in understanding how social stratification or the ranking of people in any given society by class and status came about.  Such rankings or hierarchies are often based on class, gender, ethnicity, and age and they persist over generations.

  • Class is defined as a  group of people with similar levels of wealth and income.
  • People who belong to the upper class for example are more likely to have a better education, a higher income, live in larger houses, drive expensive cars and own more goods than people in the middle or lower classes.

Origins of Social Stratification

  • According to anthropologists, the earliest forms of social stratification existed 5000 years ago in the hunting and gathering societies.
  • Composed of small groups, they were considered nomads since they consistently moved in search of games (hunting) and edible plants (gathering) in order to survive.
  • The primary bases of stratification were gender and age where men for the most part hunted meat while women gathered edible plants, and the adults held power over children. Whatever they hunted and gathered were shared among all members. 
  • As societies invented tools and materials, horticultural and pastoral groups emerged enabling the members a reliable food supply. 
  • Horticultural societies cultivated plants, while pastoral societies domesticated and bred animals. 
  • The improvement in agricultural technology eventually led to the surplus of food production which meant they could produce enough food to feed themselves as well as others. 
  • The increase in production meant greater specialization. This resulted in the creation of gaps in social status and appearance of elaborate social structures. 
  • The advent of the industrial revolution supported an even more elaborate and flexible system of stratification which impacted the development of social classes. This continued on to the post-industrial age as societies focused more on the production and consumption of services and information, rather than on  the manufacturing of goods. 

In addition to gender and age, the modern world has expanded the bases of social stratification to social classes.

 

Systems of Social Stratification

Sociologists often use two opposing standards to describe social stratification.

Closed system

  • A system with strict boundaries between different social positions.
  • Very little or no chance at all to move to another level.
  • A person stays in the class he/she was born in. 
  • Boundaries between levels are rigid. 

  • People’s positions in the closed systems are set by ascribed status where a social position is assigned to a person without regard for his or her characteristics or talents but by virtue of birth. 
  • Foremost examples are slavery and the caste system in India.

Open systems

  • Considerable flexibility is permitted and may be influenced (either positively or negatively) by achieved status where a person’s social position can be attained through his/her own effort.
  • There is social mobility where individuals can move from one level to another, either upward or downward.
  • People are given a chance to change their status through their choices.
