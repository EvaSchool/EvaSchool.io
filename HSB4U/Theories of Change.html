Theories of Change

Remember that every social change has certain unique characteristics of its own. While it can be generalized as either gradual or sudden, widespread or isolated, there are many theories to explain its process in society.

When examining societal transformation, it's crucial to recognize the unique characteristics that define each social change. While some changes unfold gradually over time, others occur suddenly and unexpectedly. Similarly, societal transformations can manifest as widespread shifts impacting entire populations or as isolated occurrences within specific communities.

To comprehend the intricate process of social change, various theories have been developed to offer insights into its underlying mechanisms and dynamics within society.

These theories encompass a spectrum of perspectives, ranging from macro-level analyses to micro-level interpretations, each shedding light on different aspects of the change process. By exploring these theories, we gain a deeper understanding of how and why social change occurs, enabling us to navigate the complexities of societal transformation with greater insight and awareness.

  • This lesson summarizes in simple terms these theories and hopes to give light on understanding the issue of social change:
    • Evolutionary Theory
    • Cyclical Theory
    • Challenge and Response Theory
    • Functionalist Theory
    • Conflict Theory

Evolutionary Theory

  • All societies evolve from simple beginnings and become more complex over time (from simple to sophisticated).
  • Adheres to the notion of progress.
  • Views change as a positive and beneficial force in society.

Cyclical Theory

  • Views change cyclically, just like a natural cycle of life that sees the birth, maturity, aging, and death of a society.
  • Suggests that a society reaches a certain level of power and relevance, and then it will die off.

Challenge and Response Theory

Arnold Toynbee (1889-1975) used this theory to explain the concept of the rise and fall of civilizations.

Challenge and Response Theory

  • Every society faces initial challenges brought about by many factors, but it has also the ability to respond successfully to these threats.
  • Successful responses to challenges make a great society.
    Reminds society not to repeat the mistakes of past cultures.

Functionalist Theory

  • Society maintains stability and social order amidst competing forces that drive change.

Auguste Comte (1798-1857) believed that aspects of social life could be understood by;

  • Social statics - the requirements necessary to maintain and achieve social order
  • Social dynamics - the factors that determine social change

Talcott Parsons (1902-1979) viewed that cultural patterns and traditions contributed to social stability.

  • Society is affected by social institutions - in pre-industrial societies, the family was the primary institution, but as society became more complex, many institutions emerged which began the process of differentiations.

Conflict Theory

  • Concerns itself with the inequities that plague society.
  • Finds its origins in the works of Marx where groups with opposing interests (rich and poor) conflict.

Ralf Dahrendorf (1929-2009) claimed that every society is subjected to constant change which brings disorganization and conflict, therefore subjecting society to constant conflict.

  • His theory goes beyond the conflict between rich and poor over wealth but from the strain between those in authority (command class) and those without authority (obey class), who are forced to follow the latter.
