Theoretical Explanations of Social Inequality

The Legacy of Marx and Weber

Any study of social inequality begins with the theories of Karl Marx and Max Weber. Both philosophers proved to be highly influential in the development of different schools of thought around the study of social inequality, particularly conflict theory.

This theory hinges on the concept that classes in society are in a never-ending state of conflict because of competition over limited resources. Thus, clashes between these groups become inevitable.

Marx examined class conflict within an economic context, between the bourgeoisie (capitalist class) who owns the means of production and the proletariat (working class).

He believed that the exploitation of the working class by the capitalists would lead to conflict between them, with the working class aiming to establish a free and classless society.

Weber developed his theories around the capitalist ideas of Marx. He believed in the importance of class as a major source of inequality and conflict but unlike Marx, he argued that social stratification cannot be solely determined by class. He identified status and power, in addition to class, and together, they contribute to social inequality.

Power is defined as the ability to exercise one’s will over others, while status group refers to people or groups who rank the same in prestige or lifestyle. It is often derived from a person’s education level, income, and profession. When combined together, they make one group superior or inferior to another group.

Functionalist Perspective of Social Inequality

For the functionalists, inequality is not only inevitable but also positive and necessary for society to properly function. Émile Durkheim was one of the more prominent proponents of functionalism. He believed that individuals see themselves as part of society’s bigger picture. An interesting point to look at is the positive contribution of inequality to the stability and functioning of society.  

The functionalists behind the explanation for social inequality are Kingsley Davis (1908-1997) and Wilbert Moore (1914-1987). Their arguments are:

  • All societies have important tasks that must be accomplished and certain positions that must be filled.
  • Some positions are more important for the survival of society than others.
  • The most important positions must be filled by the most qualified people.
  • The positions that are the most important for society and that require scarce talent, extensive training, or both, must be the most highly rewarded.
  • The most highly rewarded positions should be those that are functionally unique and on which other positions rely for expertise, direction, or financing.
  • The greater the functional importance of a position, the more rewards a society will attach to it.

In contrast to the above ideas are the arguments offered by sociologist Melvin Tumin (1919 - 1994). His arguments are summarized below:

  • The importance of a position is not always determined by a high salary or level of prestige. Take for example the salary of a Canadian Prime Minister compared with the season salary of the highest paid Toronto Raptors’ player.
  • It is difficult to explain the high salary paid to professional athletes based on how critical their job is to the survival of society.
  • Rewards and prestige are not the only means of encouraging people to fill critical positions.
  • Personal pleasure and intrinsic satisfaction also motivate people to enter particular careers.

Davis and Moore

Recognized the concept of meritocracy where ability determines the type and prestige of the job held by an individual. 

Tumin

Pointed out that ability often does not determine an individual’s success, but gender and class are better predictors of who obtains positions associated with wealth, power, and prestige.

Symbolic Interactionist Perspective of Social Inequality

George H. Mead 

  • An American sociologist who studied how people view and present themselves in social situations.
  • Introduced the symbolic interactionist perspective in the 1920s which defined the way people understand their social world .
  • His theory explained that people interact with each other by interpreting and defining their language and actions and attaching meaning to them.  

When it comes to stratification structures, the symbolic interactionists see power as relative, that it can be negotiated  between people with different capacities and interests. They see that there is mutual understanding among people that they are part of a hierarchical system and they have come to accept it.
