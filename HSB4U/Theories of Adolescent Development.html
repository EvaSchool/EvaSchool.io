Theories of Adolescent Development

The following theories of development will help us understand the tremendous changes and processes that happen during this critical period between childhood and adulthood:

  • Dominant Values (Eduard Spranger)
  • Gradual Change to Adulthood (Leta Stetter Hollingworth)
  • Field Theory of Adolescence or FTA (Kurt Lewin)
  • Other Schools of Thought on Adolescent Development

Eduard Spranger and Dominant Values

  • German philosopher and psychologist Eduard Spranger (1882-1963) devoted much of his time to researching adolescent issues.

Adolescence marks the following:

  • transition period from childhood to physiological, emotional, and psychological maturity
  • stage when lasting dominant value direction is developed where the individual commits to a world view that will be carried forward to adulthood
  • period of crisis and volatility that can lead to a shift or change in personality development

Three basic characteristics of behavior in teenagers:

  • They undergo a sort of a rebirth where upon reaching maturity, they look upon themselves as a completely changed person.

  • They come to acquire and accept society’s cultural values and ideas through a slow process of personal development.

  • Willingness to participate in their own personal growth through personal effort, self-control, and discipline.

Leta Stetter Hollingworth: Gradual Change to Adulthood

  • Attacked G. Stanley Hall idea of storm and stress.
  • Emphasized the idea of a gradual, continual, and calm development from adolescence into adulthood.
  • Refuted the idea of phases of the life cycle.
  • Described an individual’s life course as a fluid journey that could be witnessed in gradual increments from childhood to adolescence to adulthood.

Kurt Lewin’s Field Theory of Adolescence (FTA)

  • German psychologist Kurt Lewin (1890-1947) developed the field theory of adolescence which attempted to explain the individual’s behaviour without making wide-sweeping generalizations about youth as a whole.
  • Adolescence is the time of life when individuals must alter and change their social group membership since this is the stage where they straddle between the child group and the adult world.

Structural functionalism

Social structures exist to serve the needs of society and its members.

Adolescents accept their role as students in the education system, which serves the important function of transmitting knowledge, skills, and values necessary in adult life.

Conflict theory

Power (wealth) is the basis for relationships among groups and individuals in society and is the source of certain forms of social conflict.

Socio-economic status varies among teens, influencing their purchasing power, roles as consumer, and post-secondary choices.

Feminist theory

Social conflict is created by inequality related to gender issues and roles.

Female teens may face barriers to pursuing their own career paths.

Symbolic interaction

The individual’s role and function in society is determined by his/her interpretation and reaction to it.

Adolescents are faced with the developmental tasks of determining their identity and forging an individual perspective. Adolescents are learning to see themselves as more than just a member of a group (such as the family).

Social exchange

Weighs the costs and benefits of belonging to and participating in personal  relationships and social groups.

Social and peer pressure force many adolescents to conform to group behaviour without adequately weighing the benefits and costs to themselves.
