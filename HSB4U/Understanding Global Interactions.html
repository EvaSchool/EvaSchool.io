World-System Theory and Exploitation

  • Wallerstein’s world-systems theory examines the world economy as a capitalist system and its impact on global social systems.
  • Wallerstein followed some of Karl Marx’s sociological theories such as the idea that social conflict occurs among materially-based social groups and the certainty of competitive class struggles.
  • The belief in the presence of a world economic system where some benefit from the exploitation of others is central to this theory. According to Wallerstein’s world-system theory, poorer and exploited countries become dependent on the richer countries’ investment capital, technology, expertise, and manufactured goods.
  • The differences in power exist between the core, periphery, and semi-periphery nations, leading to the exploitation of the weaker ones. In this system, the exploitation between the core nations and the periphery nations is highlighted.
  • The periphery is forced to sell its products at a low price to the core, where the products are processed and sold back to the periphery at a much higher price.
  • An inevitable result of the system is the creation and maintenance of a social gap between the rich core and the poor periphery since goods to improve quality of life are inaccessible to the periphery because of their price.
  • In addition to this, poorer countries become highly indebted to the richer countries because of foreign aid, loans, and trade deficits. Sadly, this exploitation happens on a global scale.

The textbook gives a vivid example of how the world-systems theory works in the following case of how a raw material (coffee beans) from a periphery (Ethiopia) makes its way to the core (North America):

  • Beans are grown in Ethiopia and sold to coffee distributors in Mexico. The price received by Ethiopian farmers is low. The Ethiopians are the periphery, which is the most exploited group.
  • The distributors in Mexico represent the semi-periphery. They sell the coffee beans to a larger coffee company in North America, which represents the core. The Mexican distributor exploits the periphery but is also being exploited by the core.
  • The North American coffee company uses its technology and specialized processes to create the final product. The final product will then be sold in grocery stores in the
  • North American market and around the world at a significantly higher price than the cost of producing the beans on the farm.
  • The model will continue to exist as long as the three types of nations exist.&nbsp; However, the entire system will fall flat should the natural resource become unavailable or depleted.
  • In this case, without the supply of coffee beans to purchase, the distributors and coffee companies will not be able to produce a product.
  • Therefore, exploitation cannot occur without a resource to exploit.
  • To put a stop to this system of exploitation, Wallerstein and Marx both agree that the system needs to evolve into a more sustainable one based on equality and the sharing of wealth, not the accumulation of capital.
  • To ensure that wealth will be shared, countries that have natural resources (the periphery) should benefit fairly from their production and should be given the chance to learn the technology used to process the products.

Canada’s Role as a Global Citizen

Global interactions are not limited to economic, political, and cultural; it also means charity. Canada may be considered a fortunate nation, in that it is in a position to help its global neighbors when disasters strike.

The assistance Canada usually extends is in the form of volunteers, food, or other supplies. There are many instances when the country extended support to relief efforts to countries affected by natural and man-made disasters alike.

Some documented instances of Canada’s generosity are shown in the following two instances:

  • The Indian Ocean tsunami in 2004 where Canada donated $425 million to relief efforts and for purposes toward rebuilding a strong societal foundation such as building permanent housing for families, supporting vocational programs and business training, and strengthening local non-government organizations and local government agencies.
  • The 7.0 magnitude earthquake in Haiti in 2010 where Canada donated $220 million in support of Haiti’s recovery efforts which included assistance from technical personnel (engineers, medical personnel, rescuers, and military forces), among others.
