Non-economic Aspects of Globalization

Globalization is not all about the economy, but it also has a wide impact on world politics and culture.

Political Globalization - MDBs - UN, WHO, ADB (role during pandemic), IMF
  • Nation- state is a term used to describe a state or country in terms of geography, politics, and cultural identity.
    • Japan is an example of a nation-state in which the people speak the same language, practice the same religion, and share a set of national values.
    • Sovereignty is an important feature of a nation-state, which means having its system of law, currency, and bureaucracy under a recognized government.
  • The importance of nation-states under globalization is being reduced as many national functions are being replaced by international agreements. As a result, “world politics” are becoming increasingly more important than the politics of individual nation-states.
  • The Group of Eight (G8) summit is an annual meeting of leaders from eight of the most powerful countries in the world. It started as G6 in 1975 with the United States, United Kingdom, France, Italy, Japan, and Germany.
  • With the addition of Canada in 1976, it became the G7 and then finally to G8 when Russia joined in 1998. The summit tackles pressing global issues and plans what actions to take. G8 is an example of political globalization which refers to the process in which world politics is becoming increasingly integrated, resulting in the creation of organizations that collaborate on world issues.
  • The spread of infectious diseases, financial crises, and climate change, all affect the security, and prosperity of all countries and therefore call for global initiatives. The world community responds through joint resources and commitment.
  • Countries come together to form organizations to solve a specific global issue which is referred to as multilateralism.
  • The United Nations (UN), the World Health Organization (WHO), the World Bank (WB), and the International Monetary Fund (IMF) are examples of multilateral organizations created in response to global issues.

United Nations (UN)

  • Aims to maintain international peace and security, to develop friendly relations among nations, and to promote social progress.
  • Founded in 1945 after the Second World War, it has 193 members out of 196 countries. Only three countries are not members - Kosovo, Taiwan, and Vatican City - either by choice or because they do not meet the UN’s criteria for membership.
  • The UN works on a broad range of issues, from sustainable development, international health, expanding food production, and other initiatives to achieve its goals and coordinate efforts for a safer world for present and future generations.

World Health Organization (WHO)

  • It is responsible for international public health and works for the betterment of health worldwide.
  • An agency of the UN, WHO was founded in 1945 with the US and is tasked to provide the highest possible standard of health for people worldwide.
  • This includes shaping the health research agenda and stimulating the generation, translation, and dissemination of important health-related information.
  • WHO also monitors international efforts to control outbreaks of infectious diseases such as SARS, HIV-AIDS, and the COVID-19 pandemic and sponsors programs to prevent and treat such diseases.
  • It also supports the development and distribution of safe and effective vaccines, pharmaceutical diagnostics, and drugs. These are all important steps toward ensuring the betterment of health for people globally.

World Bank

  • Established in 1944 in response to rising world poverty rates, the World Bank is an international financial institution whose goal is to eliminate poverty by supporting capital development.
  • Its primary activities include promoting foreign investment and international trade in developing countries and lending money and/or interest-free credit to underdeveloped countries for programs that will develop capital.
  • Underdeveloped countries can use money provided by the World Bank to support a wide array of investments in areas such as education, health, public administration, infrastructure, financial and private sector development, agriculture, and environmental and natural resource management. As of 2012 the World Bank has 187 member countries.

International Monetary Fund Asian Development Bank (ADB)
  • Its primary goal is to foster global monetary cooperation, secure financial stability, facilitate international trade, and promote high employment and sustainable economic growth.
  • Founded in 1944, it has 187 members as of 2012. Its members agree to its policy rate policies and currency exchange arrangements supervised by the organization.
  • In doing so, member states hope to avoid the sudden and large fluctuations in currency values that may occur.
  • IMF provides a forum for policy dialogue and offers policy advice and financing to members experiencing economic difficulties.
  • A regional development bank was established on 19 December 1966, which is headquartered in the Ortigas Center located in the city of Mandaluyong, Metro Manila, Philippines. The company also maintains 31 field offices around the world to promote social and economic development in Asia.
  • The bank admits the members of the United Nations Economic and Social Commission for Asia and the Pacific (UNESCAP, formerly the Economic Commission for Asia and the Far East or ECAFE) and non-regional developed countries From 31 members at its establishment, ADB now has 68 members The Asian Development Bank (ADB) has allocated $25 million to help the Philippine government purchase vaccines for the coronavirus disease (COVID-19) in 2021.
  • The financing will come from the $125 million Health System Enhancement to Address and Limit COVID-19 project, which ADB approved on 24 August 2020. It will allow the Philippine government to pay vaccine manufacturers in advance to secure the delivery of vaccines to its citizens.

  

Globalization and Human Rights

The UN General Assembly adopted the Universal Declaration of Human Rights on December 10, 1948, as a result of the atrocities experienced during the Second World War. 

Vowing not to allow atrocities again of such magnitude proportion, the members decided to complement the UN Charter with a document that would guarantee the rights of every individual, everywhere.

It has the following articles:

  • All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights.
  • Everyone is entitled to all the rights and freedoms outlined in this Declaration, without distinction of any kind, such as race, color, sex, language, religion, political or another opinion, national or social origin, property, birth, or another status.
  • No one shall be held in slavery or servitude.
  • Slavery and the slave trade shall be prohibited in all their forms.
  • And no one shall be subjected to torture or cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment or punishment.

Unfortunately, this Declaration is not always adhered to. Globalization in theory should result in the creation of one global village where there is a free flow of capital, goods, services, and ideas. However the effect of globalization on human rights has been controversial.

   

Studies have shown that individuals in developing nations are often exploited and experience human rights violations due to the effects of globalization like illegal trade of human beings for slavery or forced labor and sex trafficking which refers to situations in which women and female children are sexually exploited.

  

Cultural Globalization Americanization
  • This type of globalization relates to how the world’s increasing interconnectedness leads to a “shared” global culture - common values, ideas, and ways of life among individuals from across the world. Many factors contribute to cultural globalization, advancements in air travel being one of them.
  • Before the COVID-19 pandemic, people were traveling more frequently and more widely than ever before. Therefore they are being exposed to different cultures, values, and ideas. We have talked about in our previous lessons how television, the mass media and communication technologies, and the Internet have become the primary instruments of bringing cultural globalization.
  • Some critics of cultural globalization are wary that individual countries are becoming fragmented, their values are beginning to be lost as growing numbers of immigrants introduce new customs, as international trade increases, and modern media communications invade every corner of the world displacing local culture. Some even see it as generating cultural homogenization or the blending of different cultural practices into one uniform cultural practice. This practice to some extent becomes cultural repression which happens when large international corporations can bring cheap goods to global markets thereby displacing local retailers.
  • Another controversial aspect of globalization is the worldwide spread and dominance of Western, or “American,” culture. This happened as a result of advances in mass communication where today’s society, music, television, film, and the Internet allow the global spread of ideas and values, most of which are Western, or American.

As a result, teenagers from around the globe become aware of whatever is American and may begin to adopt them in their own lives. Another manner of transmitting American culture is through the global spread of American corporations threatening the very core of a nation’s culture.

  • Examples are the proliferation of McDonald’s Corporation, a burger chain, and Starbucks Corporation, a coffeehouse chain, both are American multinational corporations.

They not only affect the eating habits of the locals but also influence the traditions and mores in the countries where they are located.

Take for example the case of Starbucks in Italy. There, drinking coffee is associated with socialization - a time to relax at outside cafés and chat with friends - unlike in the US where it is common for people to buy take-out coffee for drinking at home, in the car, or in the office. 

  • For the Italians, a large coffee chain like Starbucks cannot provide the personal and friendly atmosphere local coffee shops offer.
