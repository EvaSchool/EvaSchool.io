Challenges to Social Relationships in Adolescence

  • Individuals, especially adolescents have a strong need to belong to a group as well as conform to group ideals. 
  • Teenagers are said to value the concepts of group membership and conformity which is the act of matching attitudes, beliefs, and behaviours to what individuals perceive as normal to their society or social group.
  • Example of this is gang membership which appeals to them as an alternative especially when home life is stressful and problematic. It is said to overpower an individual’s rational thinking, despite knowing its negative consequences.

Festinger and Social Comparison Theory

  • This theory says that individuals routinely compare themselves with others when they are unable to judge their status and abilities on their own.
  • Leon Festinger believed that when individuals compare themselves with similar people, they tend to have more accurate information about themselves and their performance in any given field.

This theory became the basis of two types of social comparison:

  1. Downward comparison - occurs when an individual compares him/herself to someone who is worse off.

  2. Upward comparison - occurs when an individual compares him/herself to someone who is better off.

Festinger’s theory is said to become dangerous when applied to lifestyle and wealth. It drives families to spend beyond their means to keep up with appearances.

Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs

  • In trying to find the answer as to what motivates people’s need to belong, Abraham Maslow (1908-1970) looked at the motivating forces behind human behaviour.
  • Published in 1943, Maslow’s renowned hierarchy of needs theory outlined several stages of needs to help explain human motivation where the ultimate goal in life is balance or equilibrium. An individual has to meet several needs to achieve this.

In his hierarchy of needs theory, there are four layers of needs an individual has to go through before she/he realizes self-actualization.

  • The lower needs are the “deficit” needs - they are recurring and have to be fulfilled often (e.g., need to satisfy hunger and thirst).
  • The higher needs are “being” needs. These are deeper needs that rely on intellect and introspection on the part of the individual (e.g., need to recognize beauty in nature).
  • Each need has to be sufficiently met in order to progress to satisfy the higher levels of needs and ultimately allow the individual to develop his/her fullest potential as a human being. 
  • This is the point at which the person is able to transcend all other needs and experience one’s true purpose.

The In-Group and Out-Group Dynamic

  • Two questions arise when satisfying a fundamental need of social belonging: what kind of social groups individuals belong to and what does membership in these groups say about the individual. 
  • Social psychologists Muzafer Sherif (1906-1988) in his 1949 research on group dynamics provided answers to these questions.

  • Muzafer Sherif (1906-1988)
    • He came up with two types of groups: the in-group and out-group.
    • He posited that when individuals are brought together to interact for a shared purpose, they produce an in-group with social hierarchy and roles.
    • But when two groups are formed and brought together in a condition of competition, hostile attitudes and actions, will result in the creation of a second group which is called the out-group.
    • These concepts of in-group, out-group and group formation were examined in a study called the Robbers Cave experiment.
