Theoretical explanations of Deviance

 

The Functionalist Perspective on Deviance

It is one of the schools of thought in anthropology, while structural functionalism is a school of thought in sociology and a school of thought on adolescent development.

This perspective’s take on deviance is that it is a common part of human existence with both positive and negative consequences for social stability.

A negative consequence of deviance could be an erosion of trust where a society with widespread suspicion and distrust cannot function smoothly. A positive consequence on the other hand can benefit society.

Consider the case of parents losing custody of their children because of neglect, it gives an example how society expects parents to act. Deviance in this case helps to define the limits of proper behavior.

Sub-theory: Strain Theory

Robert Merton (1910-2003) developed the strain theory (conformity, ritualism, innovation, retreatism, and rebellion) that was built on Émile Durkheim’s theory of anomie, a social condition where there is lack or breakdown of social norms.

The strain theory on deviance says that it is more likely to occur when a gap exists between cultural goals and the culturally approved means of achieving these goals.

  • For example, the goals are material possessions and money; the approved means may include education and employment. When denied legitimate access to these goals, some people seek access through deviant means.

Sub-theory: Control Theory

The control theory also known as the social bond theory, argued by American sociologist Travis Hirschi (1935-2017), is also rooted in Durkheim's anomie theory. Control theory states that conformity to social norms depends on the presence of strong bonds between individuals and society. If these bonds are weak or broken (or when anomie is present), deviance occurs. This theory says that social bonds control the behavior of individuals.

Symbolic Interactionist Perspective on Deviance

It is in the process of socialization where people develop self-concepts and learn conforming behavior.

For the symbolic interactionists, deviance is learned through interaction with others. They also support the differential association theory and the labelling theory.

Differential Association Theory

This emphasizes the role of primary groups and secondary groups in transmitting deviance. According to American sociologist Edwin Sutherland (1883-1950) who developed the theory, people learn the deviant behavior from others whom they associate with. Hence, individuals have a greater tendency to deviate from societal norms when they frequently associate with people who favor deviance over conformity.

Labelling Theory

Also known as social reaction theory, it was first proposed by Howard Becker (1928-2023) and explains why certain people are viewed as deviants while others with similar behaviours are not because they have been successfully labelled as such by others. 

This labelling process theory was further clarified by American sociologist Edwin Lemert’s (1912-1996) distinction between primary and secondary deviance. Primary deviance involves the occasional breaking of norms that are not a part of a person's lifestyle or self-concept. The behaviour may be considered deviant but not the person who committed the act. Secondary deviance is behaviour that is in reaction to being labelled as deviant. These individuals label themselves as deviants as do others who identify them as deviants and so they respond to them accordingly.

Conflict Perspective on Deviance

Karl Marx extensively wrote about the conflict perspective which hinges on the concept that classes in society are in a never-ending state of conflict because they compete with each other over limited resources. Marx’s ideas influenced a critical approach to deviance on the assumption that the criminal justice system in a capitalist society protects the power and privilege of the upper class. They do this through legislation that benefits themselves but are detrimental to the working class.

Though both groups commit deviance, the system created by the capitalists defines deviance differently for each group. To wit:

Social background - ordinary people who commit fraud often face criminal charges while professionals committing fraud are often faced with a disciplinary committee of peers rather than by the criminal courts.

Racial and ethnic background - In Canada, people from the First Nations communities and from other visible minority groups are dealt with more harshly than white people.
