The Criminal Justice System

Police

The police serve as the primary point of contact between the population and the criminal justice system. They are tasked to maintain order by enforcing the laws of society in order to protect the public. Statistics Canada reported that in 2019, there are a total of 68,718 police officers in Canada (53,451 [78%] males and 15,268 [22%] females). This gives a police officer per 100,000 population of 182.8, it used to be 192 per 100,000 population in 2006. In order to do their duty of protecting the country’s 35 million population, they exercise considerable discretion about which situations warrant their attention and how to handle them.

Courts

Once an arrest by the police is done, a court determines whether a suspect is guilty or innocent. There are four major players in Canadian courts:

1.   Crown prosecutor - presents evidence to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt. They are called as such because they represent the Crown of the Monarch of Canada.

2.   Defense lawyer - challenges the Crown’s case.

3.   Judge - monitors the adherence to legal procedures.

4.   Judge or Jury - determines if the evidence warrants a guilty verdict.

The Correctional System

It is designed to punish, to deprive a person of things of value (including freedom) because of a criminal offence a person has committed.  It serves four functions:

  1. Retribution - imposes a penalty on the offender based on the premise that the punishment should fit the crime.
  2. Social protection - renders an offender incapable of further offences through incarceration.
  3. Rehabilitation - seeks to return the offenders to the community as law-abiding citizens.
  4. Deterrence - seeks to reduce criminal activity by instilling a fear of punishment.

It is believed that laws can deter crime. Its basic idea is that punishing convicted criminals will serve as an example to keep others from committing crimes.

There is an argument that the threat of punishment only deters crime if the potential lawbreakers know two things: if they are likely to get caught; and if the punishment is severe.

An alternative to the traditional justice system tries to restore social relationships rather than just punishing offenders.

Restorative justice seeks to focus on repairing the harm that was done to the victim and to the community and at the same time work on the offenders to prevent them from re-committing crimes in the future.  It is focused on inclusivity and cooperation between and among stakeholders - victims, offenders, and community - towards healing, restoration, rehabilitation, and reconciliation.

It is worth noting here that the Aboriginal communities in Canada are leading the way in the use of restorative justice practices.  They use a variety of methods like sentencing circles which bring the offender, the victim and other community members  (judge, prosecutors, families, elders) to resolve disputes. The circle symbolizes equality of all parties and the process intends to restore harmony to the community.
