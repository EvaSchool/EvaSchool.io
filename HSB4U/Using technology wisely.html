Using technology wisely

 

Canadian society is shaped by different factors and trends, such as economics, politics, health, and media. One’s work not only provides monetary remuneration but also influences daily human behavior and produces a means of self-identification. Political participation is slowly shifting among young Canadian voters in the belief that ballots can actually effect change. Technological advancements brought with it the growth and type of media outlets that give exposure to images that influence individual lifestyle choices.

The younger generation are experiencing technological advancements that were never available to the older generation, and they are here to stay. With the many advantages that come with it, a discourse on how it can be used wisely to maximize its benefits has also grown.

Teachers allow students to use technology in schools on the premise that it is for the enhancement of student learning. It is not uncommon, however, that students use technology for activities other than learning, and even overuse it to continue their socialization even during class. Engaging in social media is not only addicting but since it takes a lot of time, it can easily become a major focus in a person’s day. Reading and responding to a text may only take seconds, but the actual process for the brain to go through numerous times a day can have a significant net effect on distraction from school activities when time is added up.

Aside from providing distraction, there are several challenges as to the negative effects of technology on students. Technology has made it easier to cheat on tests and assignments through the use of instant messaging and social media such as Facebook, Twitter, and Instagram. Students have taken cheating to a more powerful level where they can easily broadcast exam questions and retrieve answers with just doing a few clicks on their smartphones or laptops.
There is also the issue of privacy. Some schools monitor what their students are doing online and may have access to personal information and browsing history.

As we learned in Unit 2 about adolescents and technology, cyberbullying has been a growing problem among Canadian youths. Statistics Canada shows that in 2014, about 17% of the population aged 15-29 have experienced cyberbullying. It was also mentioned that cyberbullying is more prevalent among the younger age group and within the homosexual/bisexual population. The children also cited their classmates as the most likely source of cyberbullying.
