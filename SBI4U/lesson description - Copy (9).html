Earth is home to millions of diverse species. A zebra is white with black stripes. The sweat glands of a cow are in its nose. Bees can see ultraviolet light. Bee pollinated flowers look different in UV light than they do in the human visual spectrum. From ants and sea stars to hummingbirds and humpback whales, from ferns and moss to pine trees and potatoes, from bacteria and blue-green algae to kelp all these different species are the result of changes in sequence, length, and organization of a molecule that is composed of four nitrogenous bases. 

  • The nucleus of each human cell contains all the human genetic material.

  • Each long strand of DNA is wrapped around proteins, which allow the chromosomes to pack tightly into the cell nucleus.

DNA 

  • Human DNA is found in the nucleus of human cells in duplicate.

  • DNA stands for Deoxyribonucleic acid

  • DNA is the hereditary material in humans and almost all other organisms.

  • Most DNA is located in the cell nucleus, but a small amount of DNA can also be found in the mitochondria.

  • The genetic material is present in the form of two copies of the 22 autosomal chromosomes and one pair of sex chromosomes (XX or XY).

  • Each autosomal chromosome is numbered by size from 1 to 22.

  • Each gene has two copies, known as alleles (identical or different). 

  • Each allele of a given gene resides at the same locus on both homologous chromosomes.


The human genome is made of 3 billion nucleotide pairs, If each nucleotide pair is drawn to span 1 mm; the human genome would extend 3200 km, far enough to stretch across central Africa. 

The “Human Genome Project” was put in place to determine the sequence of the 3 billion base pairs that make up the human genome.

  • Scientists determined how the sequences of the nitrogenous bases of human DNA are ordered on each chromosome. 

  • The size of a genome or the total number of genes in the genome is not sufficient to understand the organism’s complexity. 

  • So, scientists are now working to understand the patterns of organization and the way that these patterns produce the proteins required for human life.

Genome Components

Genome organization refers to the sequential organization of the genome.

Eukaryotic genomes are composed of coding and non-coding regions.

In humans, all the protein-coding sequences occupy less than 2 % of the genome and are functional as they code for proteins.

Genome Components

In eukaryotes, non-coding regions are filled with variable number tandem repeats (VNTRs), also known as microsatellites.

VNTRs are sequences of base pairs that repeat over and over again (for example, TAGTAGTAGTAG). The lengths of VNTRs vary, as do their positions in the genome.

Long repetitive non-coding DNA sequences are used as a defense mechanism against the shortfalls of DNA Replication.

Chromosome 22, one of the smallest human chromosomes, contains 48 × 106 nucleotide pairs that constitute about 1.5% of the entire human genome. The intron–exon arrangement of a typical gene is shown after a further tenfold expansion. 

  • Exons are all the coding regions that code for proteins, they occupy less than 2 % of the genome.

  • Introns are non-coding regions in genes and occupy 24 % of the genome. 

  • The rest of the DNA, almost 75 % of the genome, occupies the spaces between the genes. 

  • Some of this intergenic DNA includes regulatory sequences and are functional (such as promoters). 

  • The rest 50 % of the genome, consists of repeated sequences that are non-functional.

Callout

  • Variable number tandem repeats (VNTRs) or microsatellites: These are sequences of base pairs that repeat over and over again (for example, TAG TAG TAG TAG). 

  • Their length and position in the genome vary.  

  • Their number is proportional to the complexity of the organism.

  • Long interspersed nuclear elements (LINEs): are repetitive DNA sequences of about 6500 base pair lengths with undefined roles. 

  • Short interspersed nuclear elements (SINEs): are repetitive DNA sequences of less than 500 base pair lengths with undefined roles. 

  • Telomeres: are non-coding regions at the ends of chromosomes that preserve coding DNA from being lost during replication.

  • Repetitive DNA sequences are found near Centromeres; and play a role in cell division. 

Callout

Viral genome: During a viral infection, viruses can insert copies of a part of their genome in a random location of the human host cell genome. This DNA sequence is active and acts like non-coding genes. When they are passed from one generation to another, this part of the viral genome becomes a shared constant in the genome of the entire species. 

Transposons: are small sequences of DNA that can move from a chromosome and insert themselves into different chromosomes.

Pseudogenes: are mutated versions of older genes that have no more value to the genome and ensure no function within the species. They have a nucleotide sequence that is similar to an existing gene but differs in certain base pairs, which makes them unable to code for proteins.

For example: 

  • Dolphins possess hundreds of pseudogenes that are mutated versions of genes originally associated with the sense of smell. 

  • Like most mammals, dolphin ancestors lived on land and had a keen sense of smell. 

  • As dolphins adapted a marine life, their sense of smell in the air has no value.

  • As a result, mutations accumulated in these redundant genes, making them pseudogenes.
