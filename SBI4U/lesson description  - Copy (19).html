Is the Genetic Material Protein or DNA?

The topic of discussion till 1944 was which chromosomal component >>> DNA or protein carries hereditary information. 

Until 1940 proteins were considered genetic material as proteins are polymers of 20 amino acids present in larger quantities, and carry out a variety of functions.

DNA is a polymer of only 4 different deoxyribonucleotides (ATP, CTP, GTP, TTP) and is present in smaller quantities.

Griffith’s experiment

Frederick Griffith(1928) studied Diplococcus pneumoniae having two strains.

 SIII strain was virulent, possessed a lipopolysaccharide(LPS) capsule, and could kill mice by causing pneumonia and made round colonies on a culture plate.

RII strain was avirulent and lacked a lipopolysaccharide(LPS) capsule,  and appeared as rough-shaped colonies on a culture plate.

Griffith’s Conclusion 

  Griffith concluded that some transforming principle transferred from the heat-killed S strain had enabled the R strain to synthesize a smooth polysaccharide coat and become virulent. This is due to the transfer of the genetic material.

The chemical composition of DNA

DNA is a group of molecules that is responsible for carrying and transmitting hereditary materials or genetic instructions from parents to offspring.

The basic building blocks of DNA are nucleotides, which are composed of a sugar group, a phosphate group, and a nitrogen base.

The sugar and phosphate groups link the nucleotides together to form each strand of DNA.

There are 2 general types of nitrogenous bases:

Pyrimidines and Purines

Both have high nitrogen content

Pyrimidines >>>> single organic rings >>>> 3 bases are uracil (U), thymine (T), and cytosine (C)

Purines >>>> 2 ringed organic structures >>>> 2 bases are adenine (A) and guanine (G)

Chargaff’s rule

Erwin Chargaff, a biochemist discovered that the number of nitrogenous bases in the DNA was present in equal quantities.

The amount of A is equal to T, whereas the amount of C is equal to G. A=T; C=G
