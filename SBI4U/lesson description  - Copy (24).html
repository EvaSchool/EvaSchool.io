The Central Nervous System and The Peripheral Nervous System Part 1

Callout

In vertebrates, the nervous system is made up of the central nervous system (CNS) and the peripheral nervous system (PNS).

 

  • The CNS consists of the brain and spinal cord, and the PNS consists of the nerves that connect the brain and spinal cord to the body organs.  
  • The CNS acts as the control center of the body and manages physiological functions by integrating incoming sensory information from the PNS. 
  • The complexity of the human brain is what distinguishes humans from other animals and allows us to have complex behaviors, including language, reasoning, memory, personality, intellect…. 

The brain and spinal cord are surrounded by three protective membranes called the meninges :

  1. Dura mater: the external layer: that supplies a complex system of venous drainage. 
  2. Arachnoid mater: the middle layer: protects from sudden impact and mechanical pressure.
  3. Pia mater: the internal layer: production of cerebrospinal fluid.

 

The cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is a liquid that circulates:

  • Through the central canal of the spinal cord.
  • Through the ventricles of the brain.
  • Between two of the meninges (in the subarachnoid space).
  • It cushions the brain and spinal cord from jarring movements and impacts, nourishes them, and protects them from toxic substances.

Embryonic development of the brain shows the principal regions found in all vertebrate brains: 

  • The forebrain was originally associated with olfaction or the sense of smell.
  • The midbrain was primarily associated with vision. 
  • The hindbrain was mainly associated with balance.

 

Callout

The hindbrain forms: 

 

  • The medulla oblongata contains the nerve centers that deal with involuntary functions.
  • The cerebellum is responsible for voluntary movements,  balance, and fine motor control.
  • In higher mammals, a mass of fibers that connects the cerebellum to higher centers in the brain forms the pons (bridge). 
  • The medulla, the pons, and the midbrain form the brainstem; a stalk-like structure that connects the forebrain to the spinal cord. 
  • The forebrain forms the cerebrum and controls most of the sensory and motor activities.

  • The surface layer of the cerebrum is a thin layer of grey matter called the cerebral cortex.
  •  The cerebrum is divided into right and left cerebral hemispheres.
  •  It is corrugated by fissures and folds that increase the surface.

Each hemisphere is divided into:

Frontal lobe,

The two cerebral hemispheres function separately:

 

  • The left hemisphere responds primarily to sensory signals from and controls movements in, the right side of the body. 
  • The right hemisphere has the same relationship with the left side of the body. 
  • The opposite connection and control is due to the crossing nerves within the spinal cord or brain stem. 
  • Thick axon bundles, which form a structure called the corpus callosum, connect the two cerebral hemispheres and coordinate their functions.

In each hemisphere:

  • The primary somatosensory area, which registers information about touch, pain, temperature, and pressure, runs in a band across the parietal lobes of the brain. 
  • The primary motor area of the cerebral cortex runs in a band across the frontal lobe. 
  • The disproportion of size reflects the precision of the movement.
  • The cerebellum receives sensory input from muscles and joints; balance receptors in the inner ear; and the receptors of touch, vision, and hearing.
  • The inputs convey information about how the body's trunk and limbs are positioned, in which direction they are moving, and the degree to which different muscles are contracted or relaxed.
  • Output from the cerebellum to the cerebrum, brain stem, and spinal cord modify and fine-tune body movements to keep the body in balance and directed toward targeted positions in space.

 

 

The cross-section of a brain shows peripheral grey matter (body cells of the neurons) and central white matter (axons of the neurons), So what are the grey areas in the middle of the brain?

  • The thalamus forms a major switchboard that receives sensory information and relays it to the appropriate regions of the cerebral cortex. 
  • it plays a role in alerting the cerebral cortex to full wakefulness or in inducing drowsiness or sleep.

   

   

The hypothalamus is a relatively small conical area that occurs in all vertebrates: 

 

  • Regulate basic homeostatic functions of the body. 
  • Maintain body temperature by triggering reactions such as shivering or sweating. 
  • Maintain the osmotic balance of the blood by testing its composition of ions and other substances. 
  • Produces some of the hormones that are released by the pituitary gland.
  • Governs the release of pituitary hormones.

The basal nuclei are grey-matter centers positioned in the middle of the brain. They moderate the voluntary movements that are directed by motor centers in the cerebrum.

 

 

Damage to the basal nuclei can affect the planning and fine-tuning of movements. 

  • This can lead to stiff, rigid motions of the limbs and unwanted or misdirected motor activities, such as tremors of the hands.
  • Parkinson’s disease is a result of degeneration in the substantia nigra.

 

The brain is isolated from other parts of the body, the substance that enters the body and circulates in the blud are unable to cross the brain. 

 

  • This is due to endothelial cells of the capillaries in the brain, these cells have tight junctions between them that set up a blood–brain barrier.
  • It’s a selective barrier that controls the diffusion of chemicals; only some molecules are able to enter such as nutrients. 
  • It protects the brain and spinal cord from infection by viruses, bacteria, and toxic substances that may circulate in the blood.
  • Allows a more stable composition of the cerebrospinal fluid, protecting it from the wide fluctuations that occur in blood in response to meals and other factors. This maintains a stable environment for neurotransmission.
  • In case of brain infections; an immune response activates T-cells that are able to cross the barrier. 
  • The part of the hypothalamus involved in communication with the pituitary gland and the pineal gland is also not protected by the blood-brain barrier; allowing hormone secretion in the bloodstream.
  • A few molecules and ions, such as oxygen, carbon dioxide, alcohol, caffeine, nicotine, and anesthetics, can move directly across the lipid bilayer of the cell membranes by diffusion. 
  • A few other substances are moved across the plasma membrane by highly selective transport proteins such as glucose (the source of metabolic energy for the cells of the brain).

Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), 3-D ultrasound, and positron emission tomography (PET) are technologies that allow scientists to identify functions of specific brain regions in non-invasive ways by recording the brain activity during various mental and physical tasks and detecting minute increases in blood flow or metabolic activity in specific regions.

  

The spinal cord extends from the base of the brain down through a canal inside the vertebrae of the backbone.

  • It carries impulses between the brain and the PNS and contains the interneuron circuits that control motor reflexes. 
  • The spinal cord has a butterfly-shaped core of grey matter, which consists of nerve cell bodies and dendrites.
  • The grey matter is surrounded by white matter, which consists of axons, many of them surrounded by myelin sheaths.
  • Pairs of spinal nerves connect with the spinal cord at spaces between the vertebrae.
  • Afferent axons enter the dorsal root of the spinal cord and make synapses with interneurons in the grey matter. The interneurons send axons upward through the white matter of the spinal cord to the brain. 
  • Efferent (outgoing) axons from the interneurons of the brain pass downward through the white matter of the cord and make synapses with the dendrites and cell bodies of efferent neurons in the grey matter of the cord. 
  • Efferent axons exit the spinal cord through the ventral root of the spinal nerves where they carry information from the spinal cord to the peripheral muscles, organs, and glands.

The Peripheral Nervous System

The CNS works in close communication with the peripheral nervous system (PNS), which is the other main division of the nervous system. In humans, the PNS plays an important role in regulating both movement and the internal environment of the body.

Callout

The peripheral nervous system consists of 31 pairs of spinal nerves grouped according to the area of the body that they serve:

  • 8 cervical, 
  • 12 thoracic, 
  • 5 lumbar, 
  • 5 sacral, 
  • 1 coccygeal, 

     

Each pair of spinal nerves serves a different part of the body and is responsible for communicating signals between effectors and the SNC. 

  

  • The dorsal root carries sensory information to the CNS
  • The ventral root relays motor information from the CNS 
  • These roots merge to form a spinal nerve that constitutes the PNS.

    

In mammals, motor neurons exit from the spinal cord in the ventral root to carry efferent signals from the CNS to the skeletal muscles. 

 

  • The dendrites and cell bodies of the motor neurons are located in the spinal cord. Their axons extend from the spinal cord to the skeletal muscle cells they control.

 

The efferent system of the PNS is subdivided into:

 

  • The somatic system controls voluntary movements by communicating with the skeletal muscles.
  • The autonomic system controls involuntary functions by communicating with the smooth muscles and glands.

  

Each spinal group of the spinal cord is responsible for the movement of a specific body area muscles.

 

  • Although the somatic system is primarily under conscious and voluntary control.
  • some contractions of the skeletal muscles are unconscious and involuntary. 
  • This is the case of reflexes, shivering, and the constant muscle contractions that maintain body posture and balance.

 

  

  • The autonomic nervous system is organized into sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions. 
  • Both of them are always active and have opposing effects on the organs to ensure precise control. 

  

Each pathway in the autonomic nervous system uses two neurons:

  

  • The dendrites and cell body of the first neuron are in the CNS, and its axon extends to a ganglion outside. 
  • A ganglion is an enlargement of the nerve where the cell bodies of neurons are located. There, its axon synapses with the second neuron in the pathway.
  • The axon of the second neuron extends from the ganglion to the effector, which carries out the response. 
  • The sympathetic division is associated primarily with the nerves of the chest and abdomen. Just ventral to the vertebral column is a chain of ganglia “paravertebral sympathetic chain”.
  • The parasympathetic division has ganglia located within the brain, and the axons exit as cranial nerves or as the sacral nerves.

  

Everyone has experienced pain. Pain can come from a variety of sources from a superficial scratch on the skin to the trauma of a broken leg to the pain of scalding or freezing water. 

 

  • Pain can vary in intensity from mild and barely noticeable to excruciating and incapacitating. In many cases, pain is short-term and disappears in a few minutes, hours, or days. However, some conditions lead to chronic, long-term pain.
  • Pain is an interpretation by the brain of sensory input received by the substantia gelatinosa (SG). 
  • The sensation of pain is caused when SG cells are stimulated by an afferent nerve. 
  • The SG produces a neurotransmitter that communicates with the injured organ and relays a message to the brain, which interprets it as pain. 

  

 

Substantia gelatinosa (SG)

Callout

Substantia gelatinosa (SG) is a band of grey matter in the spinal cord that, when stimulated, produces a neurotransmitter that communicates with an injured region of the body; a simultaneous signal to the brain results in the perception of pain.

  • The brain then signals the release of natural painkillers or opioids “endorphins and enkephalins”.
  • These molecules are produced by the pituitary gland and the hypothalamus during pain, excitement, exercise, sexual intercourse, and even when eating spicy foods.
  • They attach to the SG cell receptors and prevent communication with the injured organ. 
  • When the situation is painful, the perception of pain is diminished. 
  • When the situation is exciting, a feeling of euphoria develops.

 

Synthetic painkillers such as heroin, codeine, and morphine have the same structure as natural opioids and act by the same mechanism to reduce pain.

  • The use of synthetic painkillers causes the body to reduce its production of natural painkillers.
  • Thus, the drug must be taken continuously to experience the constant effect of “Drug dependence”.

Opioid

Opioid drugs are often associated with substance abuse and dependence. Opium is so named because it is derived from the milky sap of the unripened seedpod of the opium poppy. Opium has been used as a painkiller for several thousand years, and it is still cultivated for both legitimate and illegitimate purposes, mainly in Myanmar and Afghanistan.

    

  • The “runner’s high” is a feeling of pleasure experienced during or after periods of intense exercise. 
  • The pleasurable sensation is due to the reduction of pain by endorphins and enkephalins. 
  • Research that used PET images of a runner’s brain has shown that running for 2 h increases the release of endorphins in specific regions of the brain.
  • Because of this effect on mood, Several studies have suggested that exercise may be just as effective as antidepressant medications.
