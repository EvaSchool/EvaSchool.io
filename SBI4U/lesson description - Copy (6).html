The human genome contains more than 20 000 genes and yet the genetic code is based on only these four nucleotide bases. Furthermore, these 20,000 genes code for 100,000 different proteins.

Messages from the genes, in the form of RNA molecules, travel to the ribosomes to direct the assembly of Amino acids that form proteins. One Gene-One Protein Hypothesis

Two key pieces of research involving defects in metabolism led scientists to the discovery of how genes encode proteins by specifying their amino acid sequence. 

First Research concluded that : 

People with Alkaptonuria 

have Black urine.

The black pigment is due to the 

presence of cumulative chemicals

in the urine called Alkapton. 

  • The inborn metabolic error is located in the catabolic pathway of an Amino acid “Tyrosine”. 

  • An enzyme responsible for the transformation of homogentisic acid to Maleyacetoacetic acid is defective. 

  • Therefore there’s an accumulation of Alkapton in the patient’s urine.

The cause is a mutation in the gene that codes for the enzyme included in the metabolic pathway of Tyrosine. 

This causes the production of an abnormal defective enzyme That can’t complete its activity.

The second Research concluded that : 

  • The mutated strain that required the addition of the amino acid arginine had a defect in a gene that coded for an enzyme required in the synthesis of arginine. 

  • The assembly of arginine from raw is a multistep process, with different enzymes responsible for each step. 

  • Therefore, different “arg” mutants differ in the enzyme that is defective and in which step that is blocked.

  • As a conclusion, the biosynthesis of arginine required a number of steps, and each step was controlled by a gene that codes for the enzyme responsible for this step. 

  • This is where the “One Gene-One Protein” hypothesis is.

  • One gene-one enzyme hypothesis The hypothesis, proposed by Beadle and Tatum, is that each gene is unique and codes for the synthesis of a single enzyme

  • One gene–one polypeptide hypothesis is the hypothesis that each gene is unique and codes for the synthesis of a single polypeptide; the restated version of the one gene-one enzyme hypothesis

Transcription & Translation

The process in which a cell can transform genetic information from DNA to Proteins is constituted of  2 mechanisms “transcription & translation” and passes through a transitory phase which is the RNA. This fundamental principle which states that genetic information flows from DNA to RNA to Proteins is called a

 Central Dogma.  

Timeline

  • Ribonucleic Acid “RNA”

    Like DNA, ribonucleic acid (RNA) is a carrier of genetic information. There are 3 types of RNA, can you rely on each type of RNA to its own description?

    ribosomal RNA (rRNA)

    • Varies in length, depending on the gene that has been copied.

    • Acts as the intermediary between DNA and Amino acids that forms proteins.

    • Is translated into protein by ribosomes.

    • Its specificity is that it can pass through the nuclear membrane.

  • transfer RNA (tRNA)

    • Is very short, only 70 to 90 base pairs long.

    • It transfers the appropriate amino acid to the ribosome to build a protein, as dictated by the mRNA template. 

  • messenger RNA (mRNA)

    • Varies in length.

    • Is a structural component that forms, with the proteins, a ribosome.

The genetic code is the specific coding relationship between bases and the amino acids they specify; it can be expressed in terms of either DNA or RNA bases. 

the RNA “alphabet” consists of the four letters A, U, G, and C. However, while there are only four RNA bases, there are 20 amino acids. 

So the four bases in the mRNA are used in combination to be able to provide the 20 amino acids. 

  • If the code used only single letters (bases), only 4 different amino acids could be specified (A, T, C, and G)

  • If the code used 2-letter combinations, only 16 different amino acids could be specified (AA, AT, TT, . . . )

  • But if the code used 3-letter combinations, 64 different amino acids could be specified (AAA, AAT, AAC,. . . ) which would cover the 20 existing amino acids.
