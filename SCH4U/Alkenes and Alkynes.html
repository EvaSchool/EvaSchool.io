Alkanes, Alkenes and Alkynes

  1. ALKANES:   

    In naming alkanes, a specific prefix indicates the number of carbon atoms and followed by the suffix –ane. 


    Alkanes have a general formula of CnH2n+2.

         

Examples of alkanes are:

Wax (candles) 

Nylons (used in parachutes)

Plastics and polymers

Gasolines

Petrochemicals

Asphalt

Petroleum jelly

Heavy oils which are used in vehicles

Properties of Alkanes:
  • Alkanes are nonpolar compounds. 
  • Alkanes exhibit low boiling points and melting points. 
  • Boiling points of alkanes depend on the length of the carbon chain; the longer the chain, the higher the boiling point. 

2. Alkenes:

These are hydrocarbons with at least one carbon-carbon double bond. The general formula of alkenes is CnH2n.

 

The simplest alkene is ethene, (C2H4) or commonly called as ethylene. This compound is used in agriculture, mainly in ripening of fruits. As you can see in the structural formula below, it has one carbon-carbon double bond. 

3. Alkynes:

These are hydrocarbons with at least one carbon-carbon triple bond. The general formula for an alkyne is CnH2n-2. 

The simplest alkyne is ethyne, also called acetylene. Acetylene is used as fuel in welding operations. 

These two pentane structures represent two different compounds because the molecule cannot rotate around the double bond. 

The two structures are called stereoisomers.

Reactions of Alkenes and Alkynes

Before we delve into this topic, let us first discuss what a functional group. A functional group is a specific group of atoms within an organic compound that affects the properties of the compound (solubility, melting point, boiling point, and chemical reactivity). 

Addition Reaction

Hydrogenation is the addition of hydrogen gas to alkenes and alkynes. This reaction results for the compound to be saturated (becomes alkane). For hydrogenation, one bond is broken in a multiple bonded carbon for every molecule of hydrogen gas.
