Nitroglycerine 

Given the instability of nitroglycerine, it is surprising that it is also used as a heart medication. Doctors use nitroglycerine to treat angina, a type of chest pain associated with clogged arteries and decreased flow of blood. 

When used as a heart medication, nitroglycerin does not explode. Instead, it undergoes a different chemical reaction, which releases small quantities of nitric oxide. Nitric oxide acts on the walls of blood vessels to make their diameter larger. This increases blood flow and relieves angina. 

Chemical Kinetics

Branch of chemistry concerned with the rates of chemical reactions

Reaction Rate

the change in concentration of a reactant or a product of a chemical reaction per unit time

Measuring Reaction Rates

For a chemical reaction, it is difficult to know exactly when the reaction is complete. Therefore, chemists track the progress of a chemical reaction by observing the appearance of a product or the disappearance of a reactant at particular time points. These observations are then used to calculate the reaction rate. 

Calculating Average Reaction Rates

Average reaction rate: the change in reactant or product concentration over a given time interval

Summary:

  • To determine the rate of chemical reactions, chemists may monitor changes in pressure, volume, color, mass, pH or conductivity. 
  • Reaction rates are not constant; they change as the reaction proceeds.
  • The average rate of reaction is the change in concentration of a reactant or product over a specified time period.
  • The instantaneous reaction rate is the rate of a chemical reaction at one specific instant.
  • By convention, reaction rates are always expressed as positive values.
  • For any chemical reaction, the reaction rates are related by the stoichiometric relationships found in the balanced chemical equation.
