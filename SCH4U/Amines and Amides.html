Amines and Amides

Amine:

An amine is a derivative of ammonia in which one or more of the hydrogen atoms are replaced with alkyl groups.

In the image on the right, the blue atom represents nitrogen and as you can see, the nitrogen atom is bonded to three other atoms.

Properties of Amines

Since nitrogen is more electronegative than carbon and hydrogen, the N-C bonds and any N-H bonds are polar. This greatly affects the properties of amines.

  • Small amines are soluble in water.
  • Amines have higher melting points and higher boiling points than corresponding hydrocarbon compounds.

Amides

Amides are structurally similar to esters, except the two chains are joined by a nitrogen atom next to a carbonyl group rather than an oxygen atom. 


Properties of Amides

Amides are somewhat soluble in water due to the -NH groups. As the length of the carbon chain increases, solubility decreases.

              

Primary amides, with nitrogen atoms each bonded to 2 hydrogen atoms, have higher melting points and boiling points than similar amides.
