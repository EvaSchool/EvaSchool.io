Aldehyde Ketone 

Aldehyde is an organic compound containing a carbonyl group that is bonded to at least one hydrogen atom. In this case, you will find the carbonyl group at the end of the carbon chain. Methanal is the simplest aldehyde, HCHO.

               

Ketone is an organic compound that contains a carbonyl group bonded to two carbon atoms. If you find a carbonyl group in the middle of the chain, the compound is ketone. 

Properties of Aldehydes and Ketones:

The carbonyl group mainly influences the properties of aldehydes and ketones. The attraction in the carbonyl group is dipole-dipole force which is weaker than the hydrogen bonds in hydroxyl groups. This interaction is the reason why aldehydes and ketones are polar molecules.

Since dipole-dipole interaction is weaker than hydrogen bonds, the boiling point of aldehydes and ketones is lower than those of similar alcohols. Aldehydes and ketones are soluble in water, but this property decreases with the increasing length of the carbon chain of the compound.

Carboxylic Acids:

This is an organic compound that contains a carboxyl group (-COOH). Carboxylic acids are weak acids. The most common example of carboxylic acid in our home is citrus fruits, which contain citric acid.

Properties of Carboxylic Acids

 

Carboxylic acids contain two polar functional groups- a carbonyl and a hydroxyl- this means that the molecules are very polar. Carboxylic acids with 5 or lower carbon atoms are very soluble in water. The solubility of carboxylic acids in water decreases with the increasing number of carbon atoms in the chain.

 

Carboxylic acids are acidic in nature and react with bases to form ionic compounds and water.

Esters

Ester is characterized by a functional group that is similar to the carboxyl group except that the hydrogen atom is replaced with an alkyl group.

 

R represents an alkyl group:

Esters are formed by the condensation reaction of carboxylic acid and alcohol. To name the ester formed, locate the alcohol part and that will be the first part of the compound name. The second part of the name comes from the carboxylic acid part. The suffix -oic is dropped and replaced with -oate.

Fats and Oils

Fats and oils are large ester molecules known as lipids. The long-chain carboxylic acid component is called the fatty acid while the alcohol component is glycerol.

 

Saponification

 

A triglyceride can be split to produce glycerol and sodium salt of the fatty acid when sodium hydroxide is added to it. The resulting sodium salt is called soap, so the reaction is called saponification. 

                    

Properties of Fats and Oils

They are insoluble in water because of the nonpolar nature of the long fatty acid chains in the triglyceride molecules. 

The shape of the fatty acids determines how tightly the lipid molecules can be packed together. This affects the melting point of the compound. The high melting point means that triglycerides are solids at room temperature. 

 

Unsaturated fatty acids have a lower melting point compared to their saturated counterpart. Triglycerides from unsaturated compounds that have cis formation are likely to be liquid at room temperature. 

 

The transform of fatty acids has melting points closer to that of unsaturated fats. These fats are not digested by our system and can lead to health problems. That is the reason for the advice to choose food with little to no trans fat.
