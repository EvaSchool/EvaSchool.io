Types of Chemical Bonds and Three-Dimensional Structure

 

Callout

Chemical Bonds

  • Electrical attraction that holds atoms or ions together in a molecular element or in an ionic or molecular compound. 

When bonds form, the atoms or ions that are joined function as a unit.

Callout

Ionic Compounds and Ionic Bonding

An ionic bond is a chemical bond between oppositely charged ions.

Ionic compounds form when an atom that loses electrons relatively easily reacts with an atom that gains electrons relatively easily. Typically, ionic compounds result when a metal element reacts with a non-metal element to form positively charged and negatively charged ions that are held together by electrostatic attraction. 

Covalent Bonding

a chemical bond in which atoms share the bonding electrons

When two hydrogen atoms are far apart, they have no interaction. As they move closer together, the attractive proton-electron force balances the repulsive proton-proton and electron-electron forces. The resulting molecule is more stable. They form a covalent bond between them.

Lewis Theory of Bonding

Lewis Structure - shows the arrangement of electrons and bonds in a molecule or polyatomic ion. 

duet rule - the observation that the complete outer shell of valence electrons when hydrogen and period 2 metals are involved in bonding

octet rule - the observation that many atoms tend to for the most stable substances when they are surrounded by 8 electrons in their valence shell.

Exceptions to Octet Rule

Molecules with central atoms that are surrounded by fewer than 8 electrons. These central atoms are said to have underfilled octets.

Callout

Valence Shell Electron-Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) Theory

These steps summarize how to predict the structure of a pure substance using the VSEPR theory:

  1. Draw the simplified Lewis structure.
  2. Count the electron pairs surrounding the central atom, including both bonded and lone electron pair, and arrange them in a way that minimizes electron-pair repulsion. Do this by putting the electron pairs as far apart as possible in a three-dimensional structure. 
  3. Place the atoms bonded to the central atom at the ends of their bonded electron pairs.
  4. Determine the name of the structure from the positions of the atoms and lone pairs of electrons.

Exceptions to VSEPR Theory

There are some exceptions in which the VSEPR theory does not accurately describe the structure of a pure substance.
