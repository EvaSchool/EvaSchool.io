Introducing Polymers and Synthetic Addition Polymers

Why Plastics?

Plastics make things lighter, stronger, more flexible, and cheaper. It is no surprise that plastics are used to manufacture so many things, from medical devices to parts of the International Space Station.

Along with the benefits of plastics, however, there are some major drawbacks. Most plastics are made from petrochemicals - a nonrenewable resource. The process of extracting petrochemicals from the ground consumes energy, generates waste, and damages local ecosystems. The manufacturing process

has similar problems. 

                                                                                                                 

Plastics are synthetic polymers. Other polymers occur naturally. Silk, spider webs, hair, muscle, cotton, and wood are all composed of polymers made by living organisms. Polymer chemists have designed many polymers that have similar properties to natural polymers.

Polymers 

They are a very large molecule that is built from monomers. In the last couple of centuries, humans began manufacturing synthetic polymers for a wide range of applications, including textile fibers, rubber, and plastics.


Monomers

A monomer is one of the repeating units that make up a polymer. 


            

Formation of Polymers


Addition Reaction is combining monomers to form a polymer. 

                                                                                                        

                                                                 

Copolymer

A type of polymer that has different types of monomers combined to form the polymer chain. They may join in an addition or condensation reaction. 


Natural Polymer

 

Natural polymers are made by all living things, from bacteria to mammals. Starch and cellulose are homopolymers of monomer glucose. These are natural polymers. 


Synthetic Polymers

      


Synthetic polymers are usually made from monomers sourced from plants or from petrochemicals.
