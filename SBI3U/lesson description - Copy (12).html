Ecological Succession

Ecological succession, the process by which the structure of a biological community evolves over time.

Succession is also the gradual change in the species composition of a community over time.

Types of Ecological Succession

1. Primary Succession

Primary succession is succession that takes place on completely barren rock or mineral deposits. The thermal energy, ash, and lava of a volcanic eruption destroy all living things and cover any existing soil, creating a site for primary succession.

Pioneer species are the first species to colonize an area during succession.

 

Example: Moss, grass, weeds, etc.

Types of Ecological Succession

2. Secondary Succession

Secondary succession is succession that occurs after an existing community has been disturbed by natural events or by human activity.

 

Callout

Types of Ecological Succession

 

  • Unlike primary succession, secondary succession takes place where soil containing organic matter and sometimes a few plants may remain after the disturbance. 
  • The plant populations in the community therefore establish more quickly than in primary succession.

Human Activity and Succession

 

Human activities can have a powerful effect on ecosystems and the way they change. This is known as human-induced succession, which can lead to changes that are often unexpected and sometimes seriously detrimental to the benefits that people derive from ecosystems.

 

 

Examples of Human Activities affecting Succession

  • Pollution of the lagoons that surround small South Pacific islands 
  • Overgrazing on pasture ecosystems
  • Desertification
  • Commercial fishing

 

Examples of Human Activities affecting Succession


Desertification and fisheries succession are examples of a more general emergent property of ecosystems. Human-induced succession can make ecosystems switch from a stability domain that serves human needs (‘okay’) to another stability domain that does not (‘not okay’).
