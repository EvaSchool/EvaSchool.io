Understanding Relative Clauses

  • A relative clause is one kind of dependent clause.
  • It has a subject and verb, but can’t stand alone as a sentence. It is sometimes called an “adjective clause” because it functions like an adjective—it gives more information about a noun.
  • Always begins with a “relative pronoun” which substitutes for a noun, a noun phrase, or a pronoun when sentences are combined.
    • Example:
      • I hate the dog. The dog bit me. = I hate the dog that bit me.
      • I like the bike. My father gave me the bike. = I like the bike that my father gave me.
Pronoun Stands For Uses
who  people substitutes for subject nouns/pronouns (he, she, we, they)
whom people substitutes for object nouns/pronouns (him, her, us, them)
whose people or things substitutes for possessive nouns/pronouns (his, hers, our, their)
that  people or things

can be used for either subject or object

can only be used in restrictive relative clauses (see below)
which things

can be used for either subject or object

can be used in non-restrictive relative clauses

can also be used in restrictive relative clauses, though some people don’t like this use

Multi-Word Verbs

  • Multi-word verbs contains a verb and a particle. They are also known as phrasal verbs

Example:

The students handed in their phones to their teacher. (The underlined in the sample sentence is a Multi-word verb. They always should be together)

  • Some multi-word verbs can be followed by a preposition

Example:

     The restaurant that his friend buy food from.
     The restaurant from which his friend buy food.

Quantifiers with Relative Clauses

  • We can use quantifiers as subjects or objects of the clause with whom or which

Sandra went to the seminar, they did not know who she was
Sandra went to the seminar, none of whom knew her. (none of whom is the subject of the clause)

Karen went to an asian restaurant, some of these foods were new to her.
Karen went to an asian restaurant, some of which she had eaten before. (some of which is the subject of the clause)

Indefinite Pronouns

  • Instead of using the person that, the thing that, the place that, and the time that. They could be replaced by whomever, whatever, wherever, and whenever.

Examples:

  • Whomever borrowed your book, will return it.
  • Whatever he says, is the truth
  • Wherever they go, people follow them
  • Whenever this happens, good fortune follows.
