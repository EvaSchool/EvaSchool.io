That's a Wrap!

Figurative language is a powerful literary tool that writers use to enhance their storytelling, engage readers' senses, and convey complex ideas in a more vivid and imaginative way.

Figurative Language
  • Figurative language is the use of ordinary sentences to refer to something without directly stating it.
  • It gives the readers clarity with what the author is trying to say.
Common Types of Figurative Language
  • Similes
  • Metaphors
  • Personifications
  • Hyperboles
  • Onomatopoeias
Simile

A simile is a figure of speech that compares two very different things for emphasis or to give clarity to the reader with the use of ‘like’ or ‘as.’.

  • It uses the prepositions like or as to connect the two thoughts.
    • e.g. As light as a feather
Metaphor

A metaphor is similar to a simile, although metaphors don’t use the prepositions “like” or “as” when comparing two statements. Metaphors only make sense when given context.

Example:

  • The exam was a piece of cake.
Personification

A personification is the use of human characteristics in describing objects.

Example:

  • The TV was talking nonsense.
Hyperbole

A hyperbole is an exaggerated statement. It is used to emphasize points.

Example:

  • I’ve told you a thousand times to fix your bed!
Onomatopoeia

An onomataopoeia is the use of a word to describe a sound.

Example:

  • The car crackled on its way to the shop.
