Defining and Non-Defining Relative Clauses Part I

In this lesson, you’ll learn how to differentiate between a defining clause and a non-defining clause.

Accordion

Select each item to learn more.

Defining Clause

Information is needed in a sentence. If the sentence makes you ask some questions, it needs more information.

Example:
Do you know the person who Susan is talking about?
The sentence is complete. Let’s try removing some parts of the sentence.

Do you know the person? 
It makes you ask “which person?” right?
...that Susan is talking about. - This is the information needed to make the sentence make sense

Non-Defining Clause

  • These are parts of the sentences that does not support the information of the said sentences.

Example:

The technician, who likes to dance, fixed my phone.
The phrase does not support the idea. Which the technician fixed the phone. Does dancing fix the phone? Nope! We could remove that part. Which it still makes sense.

The technician fixed my phone.

  • Non-defining sentences always have commas before and after the relative clause

Now that you have learned about the defining and non-defining clauses. Let us move on how to use relative pronouns “that” and “which”.

Callout

“That” and “Which”

  • These two words could be used in a sentence which could define if the sentence is a defining clause or non-defining clause. 

That is used in defining clause

Example:
I am going to play the game that I bought yesterday.
The phone that is on the desk belongs to my sister

Which is used in non-defining clause
The game, which was released last week, was used by my brother.
The phone, which is colored blue, is on top of the desk
