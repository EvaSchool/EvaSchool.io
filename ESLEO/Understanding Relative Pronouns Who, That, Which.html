Understanding Relative Pronouns: Who, That, Which

Relative pronouns introduce relative clauses. The most common relative pronouns are who, whom, whose, which, that. 

The relative pronoun we use depends on what we are referring to and the type of relative clause.

There are three relative pronouns that we are going to use in this lesson and these are: Who, That, and Which. 

These pronouns are used depending on what we are referring to and the type of relative clause.

Callout

Relative Pronoun: That vs. Which 

  • In a defining clause, use that.
  • In non-defining clauses, use which.
  • Remember, which is as disposable as a sandwich bag. If you can remove the clause without destroying the meaning of the sentence, the clause is nonessential and you can use which.

To understand when to use that or which, it’s important to understand clauses. A defining clause (also called an essential clause or a restrictive clause) gives information essential to the meaning of the sentence. That is used in defining clauses.

Here’s an example: 

My water jug that has a leak is now in the storage room. 

Which introduces non-defining clauses. Unlike defining clauses, non-defining clauses (also called nonessential or non-restrictive clauses) don’t limit the meaning of the sentence. You might lose interesting details if you remove them, but the meaning of the sentence wouldn’t change. Sometimes, these phrases are set off by commas.

Here's an example:

The electric fan, which I only use in the morning, is now broken.

To create a more advanced sentences, we use relative clauses. 

To be more specific, relative clauses also called as adjective clauses can be used to join two sentences or to give more information about something. 

In this sentence, “Do you know the guy who gave her flowers?,” 
the “who gave her flowers” is what we call the relative clause.

Stylized Quote

A relative clause always follows the noun it modifies. 

For example:

Students who listen to music when studying is more productive. 

Which students is more productive? Students who listen to music.
