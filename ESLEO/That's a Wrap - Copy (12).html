That's a Wrap!

  • A relative clause is one kind of dependent clause.
  • It has a subject and verb, but can’t stand alone as a sentence. It is sometimes called an “adjective clause” because it functions like an adjective—it gives more information about a noun.
  • Always begins with a “relative pronoun” which substitutes for a noun, a noun phrase, or a pronoun when sentences are combined.
  • Multi-word verbs contains a verb and a particle. They are also known as phrasal verbs. Some multi-word verbs can be followed by a preposition.
  • We can use quantifiers as subjects or objects of the clause with whom or which.
  • Instead of using the person that, the thing that, the place that, and the time that. They could be replaced by whomever, whatever, wherever, and whenever.
