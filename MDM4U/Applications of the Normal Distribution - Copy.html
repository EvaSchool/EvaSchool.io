Applications of the Normal Distribution

Example

The College of Nursing desires to accept only the top 20% of all graduating seniors based on the results of a national placement test. This test has a mean of 500 and a standard deviation of 100. Find the cut-off score for the exam. Assume the variable is normally distributed.

Solution: Since the test scores are normally distributed, the test value (x) that cuts off the upper 20% of the area under the normal distribution curve is desired. This area is shown.

Work backward to solve this problem.

Step 1. Subtract 0.2000 from 0.5000 to get the area under the normal distribution between 500 and x

0.5000  -  0.2000 = 0.3000

Step 2. Find the z value that corresponds to an area of 0.3000 by looking up 0.3000 in the area portion under the normal curve. The required z value is 0.84.

Step 3. Substitute in the formula z=x−μ/σ and solve for x.
0.84=x−500/100
x=(0.84)(100)+500= 584
Thus, 584 should be used as a cut-off score. Anybody scoring below 584 should not be admitted. 

Example

For a medical study,  a researcher wishes to select people in the middle 60% of the population based on blood pressure. If the mean systolic blood pressure is 110 and the standard deviation is 7, finding the upper and lower readings would qualify people to participate in the study. 

Solution: Assuming that blood pressure readings are normally distributed, the cut-off points are shown.

Note that two values are needed, one above the mean (to the right of x), and one below the mean ( to the left of x). First, we need to find the value of z to the right of the mean. The closest value of z for an area or probability 0.3000 is 0.84 Substituting in the formula of x = zσ +μ, we obtain,
x = zσ +μ =(0.84)(7)+110=115.88
On the other side, z=-0.84; hence, x = zσ +μ =(-0.84)(7)+110=104.12.
Therefore, the middle 60% will have blood pressure readings of 104.12<x<115.88.
